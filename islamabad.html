Islamabad

1,301,130 Resident

Pakistan

33° 44' 16.9620'' N and 73° 5' 4.1568'' E

Population Characteristics:

Islamabad, the capital city of Pakistan, is a key hub for administration, politics, and culture. Its population is a rich mix of ethnicities and languages, as people from across the country move to the capital for education, work, and government service. The majority of residents are Punjabi, particularly those speaking the Pothohari dialect, alongside significant communities of Pashto, Urdu, and Hindko speakers. Smaller groups include Kashmiri, Saraiki, and other minorities. This diversity is reflected in daily life, with Punjabi (Pothohari) and Urdu widely used in both formal and informal settings.

Islamabad is predominantly Muslim, with over 95% of the population adhering to Islam. Minority religious communities, including Christians and Hindus, also form part of the city’s social fabric.

The city has a young, educated, and dynamic population, with high literacy rates compared to other regions of Pakistan. Civic engagement, education, and professional growth are highly valued, supported by Islamabad’s well-planned urban layout and abundant green spaces. These features contribute to a high quality of life and a socially cohesive, inclusive community.

City's Unique Identity:

Islamabad blends traditional Pakistani heritage with modern urban life. The city celebrates arts such as Punjabi and Pashto folk music, classical performances, and contemporary literature and visual arts. Institutions like the Lok Virsa Museum showcase Pakistan’s diverse crafts, textiles, and performing arts, highlighting both national traditions and the city’s cosmopolitan character.

Several notable landmarks reflect Islamabad’s unique blend of modern design and cultural heritage. These include the Faisal Mosque, a striking architectural symbol of the city and one of the largest mosques in the world, the Pakistan Monument, representing national unity and history, Daman-e-Koh, a scenic viewpoint overlooking the city, and the Lok Virsa Museum, which preserves Pakistan’s diverse cultural and artistic heritage. Together, these landmarks highlight Islamabad’s identity as both a modern capital and a center of cultural significance.

Several cultural and national festivals reflect Islamabad’s vibrant social life and traditions. These include Pakistan Dayand Independence Day, celebrated with parades, ceremonies, and public events. The city also hosts Lok Virsa Cultural Festival, which showcases traditional music, crafts, and performances from across Pakistan. These festivals highlight the city’s commitment to preserving cultural heritage while promoting community engagement and national pride.

Islamabad offers a diverse culinary scene that reflects the city’s ethnic and cultural variety. Traditional Punjabi, Pashto, and Kashmiri dishes are widely enjoyed, including specialties such as Biryani, Nihari, Chapli Kebab, and Pulao. Street food and modern cafes provide a blend of local flavors and international cuisine, catering to both residents and visitors. The city’s food culture highlights Pakistan’s rich gastronomic heritage while embracing contemporary dining trends.

Islamabad is a multilingual city that reflects the diversity of its residents. The most widely spoken language is Punjabi, particularly the Pothohari dialect, followed by Urdu, which is used as the national language and in formal communication. Other languages spoken in the city include Pashto, Hindko, Kashmiri, and Saraiki, reflecting the ethnic variety of the population. This linguistic diversity contributes to the city’s rich cultural fabric and vibrant social life.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Islamabad is the administrative, political, and cultural heart of Pakistan, hosting the federal government, key ministries, and foreign embassies. The city also supports education and innovation through its universities and research centers. Regionally, Islamabad connects northern Pakistan with the rest of the country, brings together diverse communities, and promotes economic and technological development through initiatives like special economic zones and innovation hubs. It plays a vital role in stability, coordination, and growth within Pakistan and South Asia.

City Development Focus:

Islamabad is improving its livability through ongoing urban development efforts led by the Capital Development Authority. The city is enhancing public spaces, upgrading infrastructure, and expanding environmentally focused initiatives to support a cleaner and healthier urban environment. Recent projects include the development of pedestrian-friendly areas, improvements in waste management, and the preservation of green spaces across the capital. These initiatives aim to strengthen urban mobility, promote recreation, and maintain Islamabad’s reputation as one of Pakistan’s most orderly and environmentally conscious cities.

Islamabad is implementing several innovative projects to enhance urban life, tourism, and public spaces. A flagship initiative is the pedestrian-only Food Street in Blue Area, which will span approximately 800 meters and feature a variety of dining outlets, walking spaces, and digital installations. The project is designed to provide residents and tourists with a safe, modern, and interactive culinary experience.

In addition, existing food streets such as Melody Food Street are being upgraded with improved lighting, parking, and food quality inspections by the Islamabad Food Authority to ensure safety, hygiene, and visitor satisfaction.

Other recreational and tourism-focused initiatives include the development of cable cars, zip lines, Safari Park, cultural centers, and modern horticultural gardens. These projects aim to provide world-class public spaces and recreational opportunities for residents and visitors alike

The pedestrianization of Blue Area also promotes green, walkable urban spaces, digital information services, and modern urban aesthetics, positioning Islamabad as a city that balances heritage, lifestyle, and innovation.

Islamabad’s recent urban and tourism projects, including the Blue Area pedestrian Food Street and other recreational initiatives, bring multiple benefits for residents and the environment.

  • Improved Quality of Life: Pedestrian-friendly streets, parks, and recreational spaces encourage outdoor activities, social interaction, and community engagement.
  • Environmental Benefits: Reduced traffic in pedestrian zones lowers air pollution and noise, while green spaces and gardens enhance biodiversity and air quality.
  • Economic Opportunities: Tourism and food street initiatives create jobs and support local businesses, artisans, and entrepreneurs.
  • Cultural Enrichment: Preservation of cultural landmarks and museums strengthens awareness of Pakistan’s heritage and fosters civic pride.
  • Sustainable Urban Development: Eco-friendly planning, modern public spaces, and digital infrastructure promote a sustainable and citizen-centered city.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Islamabad and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties, especially given Islamabad’s current urban transformation under the Capital Development Authority (CDA).

Joint academic programs and city‑planning research, leveraging Islamabad’s experience with the 800 m pedestrian “Food Street” in Blue Area.

Capacity‑building workshops for urban planners and local government on sustainable infrastructure and public‑private partnerships, based on CDA’s PPP model.

Revitalisation of the Art & Craft Village at Shakarparian: CDA has approved re‑planning to support artisans, studios, showrooms, café spaces, and more.

Collaborative cultural fairs, design markets, and craft entrepreneurship programs drawing on Islamabad’s artisan base and Bandung’s / Jakarta’s creative industries.

Joint development of tourism circuits combining Islamabad’s new recreational projects theme park, Ferris wheel, and cable car with cultural tours in Bandung or Jakarta.

Cultural exchange initiatives: cross-city festivals and artistic residencies leveraging Islamabad’s revived Shakarparian Cultural Complex.

Knowledge-sharing on pedestrian-first urban design: Islamabad’s car-free Blue Area as a model for walkable city planning.

Mobility collaboration: electric-cart system for pedestrians (especially elderly / children) in Blue Area.

Green & eco-tourism cooperation: cable car project from Daman-e-Koh to Saidpur, which CDA is planning as a major tourism and ecological initiative.
