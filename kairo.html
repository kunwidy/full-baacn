Cairo

23.074.200 Resident

Egypt

30°02′40″N 31°14′09″E

Population Characteristics:

Cairo, the capital of Egypt, is a dynamic and densely populated metropolis shaped by migration, urbanization, and economic opportunity. The majority of residents are ethnically Egyptian, reflecting the country’s national composition. Arabic is the dominant language spoken in daily life, while English and French are widely used in education, government, and business.

Cairo is predominantly Sunni Muslim, with a notable Christian minority, mainly Coptic Orthodox, contributing to the city’s religious and cultural diversity. The population is relatively young, with many residents moving from rural areas to pursue employment, education, and entrepreneurial opportunities. This combination of youth, cultural diversity, and rapid urban growth makes Cairo a vibrant, cosmopolitan, and socially dynamic city, central to Egypt’s political, economic, and cultural life.

City's Unique Identity:

Cairo blends Egypt’s ancient heritage with a vibrant modern cultural scene. The city celebrates traditional music, folk dances, and classical Arabic arts, alongside contemporary theater, cinema, and visual arts. Cultural institutions such as the Egyptian Museum, Cairo Opera House, and Manial Palace Museum showcase Egypt’s artistic, historical, and cultural legacy. These venues highlight both the city’s deep-rooted traditions and its role as a cosmopolitan center of creativity and cultural exchange.

Cairo is home to several iconic landmarks that reflect its historical, cultural, and political significance. These include the Great Pyramids of Giza and the Sphinx, symbols of Egypt’s ancient civilization, the Egyptian Museum which houses priceless antiquities, Cairo Tower offering panoramic city views, and Al-Azhar Mosque, a center of Islamic learning. Together, these landmarks define Cairo as a city where ancient heritage and modern urban life coexist, attracting visitors and scholars from around the world.

Cairo hosts a variety of cultural and national festivals that celebrate Egypt’s rich heritage and contemporary arts. Key events include the Cairo International Film Festival, showcasing regional and global cinema, the Cairo International Book Fair, promoting literature and education, and Religious and National Celebrations such as Ramadan and Independence Day, which feature public events and community gatherings. These festivals foster cultural pride, artistic expression, and community engagement throughout the city.

Cairo offers a rich culinary landscape that reflects Egypt’s diverse food traditions. Popular dishes include koshari (a mix of rice, lentils, and pasta), ful medames (stewed fava beans), ta’ameya (Egyptian falafel), and molokhia (leafy green stew). Street food is vibrant, with snacks such as hawawshi (spiced meat in bread) and sambousek (savory pastries). Cafés and restaurants also serve modern and international cuisine, making Cairo a dynamic hub of traditional and contemporary culinary experiences.

In Cairo, Arabic is the dominant language, with Egyptian Arabic widely spoken in daily life. English and French are commonly used in education, business, and government, reflecting the city’s multilingual and cosmopolitan character. This linguistic diversity allows residents to maintain cultural traditions while engaging in modern professional, academic, and social activities.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Cairo serves as Egypt’s political, cultural, and economic hub, hosting the Presidency, key ministries, national institutions, and diplomatic missions. The city drives governance, administration, and policy implementation while supporting education, research, and cultural development.

Regionally, Cairo occupies a strategic position in North Africa and the Middle East, connecting Egypt to Africa, the Arab world, and international markets through major road, rail, and air networks. The city contributes to regional trade, cultural exchange, and diplomatic engagement, making it central to national stability, economic growth, and Egypt’s influence within the broader Middle Eastern and African context.

City Development Focus:

Cairo is working to enhance urban livability through initiatives that improve infrastructure, public services, and environmental quality. Efforts focus on expanding access to clean water, improving waste management, and upgrading sanitation systems. The city is also developing public parks, pedestrian zones, and recreational spaces to promote healthier lifestyles and community interaction. These initiatives aim to create a cleaner, safer, and more sustainable urban environment for Cairo’s residents.

Cairo is pursuing several forward‑looking policies to enhance sustainability, mobility, and urban resilience:

  • Green City Action Plan : Cairo launched a national “Green City” strategy in partnership with the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development to integrate electric vehicles into public transit, manage private car usage, and boost climate resilience.
  • Electric Public : As part of its climate and air-quality goals, Cairo is piloting a fleet of 100 electric buses to modernize its public transport and reduce emissions.
  • Waste Management Innovation : The city is developing an Integrated Waste Management Facility in the 10th of Ramadan City, capable of handling municipal, industrial, medical, and construction waste. This infrastructure is part of a broader “Greater Cairo Air Pollution and Climate Change” project. Cairo’s long-term development plan (JICA master plan) also highlights source separation (3R: reduce, reuse, recycle) and increased recycling and composting.
  • Smart Cities & Digital Governance : Egypt’s “Smart Cities” strategy, adopted at the national level, supports smart city infrastructure in Cairo from digital services, remote healthcare, to resource-efficient systems. The city is also scaling up its Green Urbanism efforts under a national plan launched at the World Urban Forum.
  • Sustainable Urban Mobility : Major transit investments include the Cairo Monorail, which will provide high-speed, low-emission intercity rail connections. The Light Rail Transit (LRT) system extends to new developments and improves connectivity across Greater Cairo.
  • Community‑Driven and Eco‑Design Urban Planning : Architecture students from the American University in Cairo are working with river island communities (Qursaya Island) to design resilient, environmentally friendly master plans using recycled materials.

Cairo’s recent policies and initiatives in sustainable mobility, waste management, and urban planning bring multiple benefits for residents and the environment:

  • Improved Quality of Life: Expanding public parks, pedestrian zones, and modern transit systems encourages outdoor activity, reduces travel time, and enhances social interaction.
  • Environmental Benefits: Electric buses, the monorail, and integrated waste management systems reduce air pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and urban waste accumulation, while promoting recycling and resource efficiency.
  • Economic Opportunities: Investments in green infrastructure, smart city technology, and sustainable transport create jobs, support local businesses, and stimulate innovation in urban services.
  • Cultural and Social Enrichment: Public spaces, cultural centers, and community-focused urban projects strengthen civic pride, promote social cohesion, and facilitate cultural engagement.
  • Sustainable Urban Development: The city’s eco-conscious urban planning, smart city initiatives, and renewable energy adoption contribute to long-term resilience, efficient resource use, and climate adaptation.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cairo and Bandung have strong potential to collaborate in areas of sustainable urban development, culture, and innovation, building on both cities’ ongoing initiatives and expertise:

  • Smart & Sustainable Urban Development : Both cities can exchange knowledge on smart city infrastructure, green architecture, sustainable mobility, and waste management systems. Joint pilot projects could focus on electric public transport, integrated waste recycling, and eco-friendly urban planning, enhancing sustainability and urban resilience in both cities.
  • Cultural and Educational Exchange : Collaboration could include student exchange programs, joint cultural festivals, and art exhibitions, as well as language and academic partnerships, promoting mutual understanding and long-term ties.
  • Entrepreneurship & Innovation : Cairo and Bandung can establish joint startup accelerators, innovation challenges, and knowledge-sharing programs. Focus areas could include ICT, creative industries, renewable energy, and smart city solutions, connecting youth entrepreneurs from Africa and Asia.
  • Tourism Development : The cities can develop city-to-city tourism networks, sharing expertise in sustainable tourism, cultural heritage promotion, and urban event management. Cairo’s experience with large-scale urban development and cultural projects can inform joint initiatives in Bandung.
  • Youth & Community Engagement : Both cities can foster volunteer programs, civic participation, and community-driven urban projects, enhancing youth involvement and social cohesion in local neighborhoods.

Cairo, Egypt’s sprawling capital, is set on the Nile River. At its heart is Tahrir Square and the vast Egyptian Museum, a trove of antiquities including royal mummies and gilded King Tutankhamun artifacts. Nearby, Giza is the site of the iconic pyramids and Great Sphinx, dating to the 26th century BC. In Gezira Island’s leafy Zamalek district, 187m Cairo Tower affords panoramic city views.
