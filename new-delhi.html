New Delhi

33.800.000 Resident

India

28’61° LU 77’23° BT

Population Characteristics:

Delhi, India's vibrant capital, is a world within itself. With its kaleidoscope of scents, sounds, and sights, Delhi never fails to captivate. From its rich history and architectural marvels to its delectable cuisine and vibrant nightlife, the city offers an unforgettable experience.

New Delhi is a metropolitan city home to people from diverse ethnic, linguistic, and cultural backgrounds across India. This blend of traditions reflects India's "unity in diversity”. The city is one of the most densely populated in the world, with rapid population growth, driven in part by migration. This contributes to complex social dynamics and infrastructure challenges. Despite its modernity, the influence of the traditional Hindu caste system is still evident in Delhi's social structure.

The city's population has a significant percentage of upper castes, but also includes a variety of other social and economic groups. Social disparities between the various economic classes are also evident. Life in New Delhi encompasses the coexistence of a rich cultural heritage (such as traditional crafts and historical sites) and a modern and dynamic metropolitan lifestyle, including a vibrant nightlife.

City's Unique Identity:

New Delhi's major cultural icons include a number of historic monuments and UNESCO world heritage sites that reflect the city's long history under various empires, especially the Mughals. Delhi’s cultural identity is a fusion of its long, layered history and its vibrant present. As one of India’s most ancient regions, Delhi weaves together Mughal, Persian, Timurid, and Hindu traditions into its heritage.
Crafts such as pottery, enameling, and gold-thread embroidery (zari work) remain integral to its cultural tapestry, reflecting the artisanal legacy of past empires. The city’s neighborhoods carry this duality — Old Delhi, with its narrow winding lanes and traditional markets, preserves classical values; while New Delhi, with broad boulevards and modern infrastructure, represents a thriving, contemporary capital.

New Delhi has many landmarks, including the Red Fort, Qutub Minar, Humayun's Tomb, India Gate, and the Lotus Temple. Other notable landmarks include Jama Masjid, Rashtrapati Bhavan, Akshardham Temple, and Lodhi Gardens.

Historical Monuments:

  • Red Fort: A historic fort and UNESCO World Heritage site built by the Mughals
  • Qutub Minar: A 12th-century minaret that is India's tallest brick minaret and part of a UNESCO World Heritage Site
  • Humayun's Tomb: A 16th-century Mughal tomb and a UNESCO World Heritage site
  • India Gate: A war memorial and a prominent archway
  • Jama Masjid: One of the largest mosques in India

Modern and Religious Landmarks:

  • Lotus Temple: A modern and distinctive Baháʼí House of Worship known for its lotus flower design
  • Akshardham Temple: A large Hindu temple complex
  • Rashtrapati Bhavan: The official residence of the President of India

New Delhi has many festivals, including religious festivals such as Diwali (Festival of Lights), Holi (Festival of Colors), Eid ul-Fitr , and Lohri , as well as cultural and arts festivals such as Phoolwalo-Ki-Sair , Street Art Festival , Jashn-e-Urdu Heritage Festival , and Grub Fest (a culinary festival). There are also nature festivals such as the Tulip Festival.

New Delhi's culinary scene is very diverse, ranging from street food such asFrom chaat , golgappa (pani puri), and chole bhature , to curries like dal makhani and butter chicken , there are also various types of parathas (filled flatbreads) and kebabs , as well as adaptations from other countries like chole , momos , and samosas

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

New Delhi, as part of the Delhi National Capital Region (NCR), is central to one of India’s largest economic agglomerations. The NCR spans multiple jurisdictions (Delhi plus adjacent states), yet functions effectively as a single economic entity and labor pool.

Despite political fragmentation, it is economically integrated: the core (Delhi NCT) remains influential, but peripheral districts are increasingly attracting investment and contributing strongly to regional GDP. Over time, job growth and economic activity have shifted outward from the core into peripheries, indicating a decentralizing trend. This dynamic makes Delhi NCR not just a national power center, but a regional economic engine where growth and development must be planned at the metropolitan / regional scale.

City Development Focus:

The tourism sector is a vital part of the Indian economy, contributing $143 billion to GDP by 2023, according to Investindia.gov.in. It also creates jobs for millions of people, with tourism employment reaching 88 million by 2023. The government estimates that this sector could contribute USD 350 billion to GDP by 2030. This will create millions of new jobs and boost economic growth.

In 2024, India saw a strong rebound in inbound tourism, with international tourist arrivals (ITAs) reaching 20.57 million, an 8.9% increase from the previous year and a 14.8% rise compared to 2019, the pre-pandemic level. This recovery underscores renewed confidence among Non-Resident Indians (NRIs), whose arrivals surged to 10.62 million a 13.2% increase year-on-year and a remarkable 52% above 2019 levels. Leisure, holiday, and recreation remained the dominant motivations for visits, accounting for 45% of total foreign tourist arrivals. Moreover, exit data indicates that December 2024 was the peak month, with over one million foreign tourist arrivals, highlighting New Delhi’s appeal during the winter festive season.

New Delhi has some excellent policies and innovations, especially in addressing major urban challenges such asair pollution and waste management , as well as in the development of digital and health technologies.

  • 1. Gradual Response Action Plan (GRAP)
    A phased response system that includes a ban on certain construction activities, closure of stone crushing plants, and hybrid learning modes in schools when air quality reaches hazardous levels.
  • 2. Waste-to-Energy Plants
    New Delhi operates facilities such as the Timarpur Okhla waste processing plant which claims to convert thousands of tons of waste into electrical energy daily, although it still faces environmental challenges.
  • 3. Digital Economy Partnerships
    The government is actively strengthening strategic partnerships in the areas of digital economy, artificial intelligence (AI), and digital identity, both domestically and through international cooperation.
  • 4. Health Infrastructure
    Health standards in Delhi are generally above the Indian national average, with many large hospital facilities run by the central government or the capital territory, as well as provision of allopathic services and traditional medicine such as Ayurveda.

1. Climate City Networks / International Collaboration
Delhi is part of global networks for climate action, such as C40, which provides a platform to collaborate with other major cities on pollution reduction, green infrastructure, and public health.

2. Heritage-led Urban Renewal
The Hazrat Nizamuddin Basti Cultural Revival Project is an example of combining heritage conservation, community development, and environmental sustainability. This could offer lessons and partnership opportunities for other historic cities (including those in Asia–Africa) that share heritage challenges.

3. Green Jobs & Circular Economy
As part of its resilience pledge, Delhi is investing in circular economy infrastructure (e-waste eco-parks) and decentralized renewable energy, which could create green job opportunities and be an example for other cities looking to green their economies.
