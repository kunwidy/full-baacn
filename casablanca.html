Casablanca

4.012.310 Resident

Morocco

33°32′N 7°35′W

Population Characteristics:

Casablanca has a young, diverse, and rapidly growing population shaped by migration, economic opportunity, and urbanization. The city reflects Morocco’s national ethnic composition, with a majority of Arab‑Berber residents alongside a significant and growing number of sub‑Saharan migrants contributing to its multicultural social landscape. Darija (Moroccan Arabic) is the primary language spoken in daily life, while French is widely used in education, administration, and business, giving the city a multilingual character.

Islam is the dominant religion, primarily Sunni, forming the core of the city’s cultural and social identity, while Christian and other minority communities also contribute to Casablanca’s diversity. The population is predominantly youthful, with many residents moving from rural areas and smaller towns to pursue jobs, education, and entrepreneurial opportunities. This combination of youth, cultural diversity, and social mobility shapes Casablanca into a dynamic, modern, and economically vibrant urban center.

City's Unique Identity:

Casablanca blends Morocco’s rich traditional heritage with a dynamic modern cultural scene. The city celebrates iconic arts such as Andalusian and Gnawa music, traditional Moroccan craftsmanship in textiles, ceramics, and woodwork, alongside contemporary painting, sculpture, and performing arts. Cultural institutions like the Mohammed V Theatre, Villa des Arts, and the Habbous district showcase Morocco’s artistic and architectural traditions, highlighting both the country’s cultural identity and Casablanca’s emerging cosmopolitan character.

Several notable landmarks in Casablanca highlight its historical, cultural, and architectural significance. These include the Hassan II Mosque, one of the largest mosques in the world and an iconic symbol of Moroccan craftsmanship and Islamic architecture. The Old Medina preserves the city’s traditional heritage with narrow streets, souks, and historic buildings. The Mohammed V Square serves as a civic and cultural center, surrounded by French colonial-era architecture and public institutions. The Casablanca Cathedral (Sacré-Cœur), a former Roman Catholic church, reflects the city’s colonial and architectural history. Additionally, modern landmarks such as Twin Center Towers and the Anfa District showcase Casablanca’s role as Morocco’s economic hub. Together, these landmarks define Casablanca’s identity as a city that blends tradition, governance, and modern urban life.

Casablanca hosts a vibrant array of cultural and artistic festivals that celebrate Morocco’s heritage and contemporary creativity. Key events include the Casablanca International Film Festival, showcasing national and international cinema, and the Jazzablanca Festival, featuring world-class jazz, fusion, and contemporary music performances. The city also celebrates traditional Moroccan arts through events in the Habbous and Old Medina districts, highlighting music, crafts, and gastronomy. These festivals foster cultural exchange, support local artists, and strengthen community engagement, reflecting Casablanca’s dynamic cultural landscape.

Casablanca’s culinary scene reflects Morocco’s rich gastronomic heritage and its cosmopolitan urban character. Traditional dishes include tajine, slow-cooked stews with meat, vegetables, and aromatic spices, and couscous, often served with seasonal vegetables and meat. Coastal influences are seen in fresh seafood preparations, such as grilled fish and fish tagines. Street food staples include briouats (savory pastries), harira (traditional soup), and sfenj (Moroccan doughnuts). French culinary traditions are also integrated into cafés and bistros, offering pastries, breads, and coffee culture. This blend of traditional, coastal, and modern influences makes Casablanca a vibrant city for both local and international gastronomy.

In Casablanca, Darija (Moroccan Arabic) is the primary language spoken in daily life, markets, and informal communication. French is widely used in education, administration, business, and formal settings, reflecting Morocco’s historical ties and bilingual governance. Additionally, Berber (Amazigh) languages are spoken by minority communities, while sub-Saharan migrant populations contribute languages such as Wolof, Bambara, and French-based creoles. This multilingual environment gives Casablanca a rich linguistic diversity that supports both local identity and international interaction.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Casablanca in Economy:

  • Home to the Casablanca Stock Exchange, one of Africa’s most important financial centres (2nd biggest stock market in Africa).
  • A continental industrial hub for aircrafts & cars.
  • Makes Morocco ranked among the top 20 countries in manufacturing and exporting cars in the world.
  • "Al-Boraq“, the fastest train in Africa and among the fastest trains in the world.

Casablanca serves as Morocco’s economic and commercial heart, playing a central role in national development as documented by official sources including the Government of Morocco and Haut-Commissariat au Plan. The city hosts major financial institutions, corporate headquarters, industrial zones, and the country’s largest port, making it the primary hub for trade, industry, and investment. Casablanca also supports education, research, and cultural activities, contributing to Morocco’s social and economic modernization.

Regionally, Casablanca occupies a strategic position along the Atlantic coast, serving as a gateway for international trade and connecting Morocco to Europe, West Africa, and global markets. Its port, airport, and transport networks facilitate logistics, commerce, and regional integration. Through its economic leadership, infrastructure, and connectivity, Casablanca plays a vital role in national stability, growth, and Morocco’s engagement in regional and international economic networks.

City Development Focus:

City Development Focus toward a Liveable and Innovative City:

source: https://www.railway-technology.com/projects/casablanca-tramway/

  • 1. Urban Mobility
    • a. Casablanca Trainway
      b. Casablanca Busway
  • 2. Green and Public Spaces
    • a. Expansion of Ain Diab Corniche for public leisure and tourism
      b. Rehabilitation of Arab League Park
  • 3. Digital Transformation (Smart City)
    • - Smart parking and waste management systems
      - Digital city administration (e-services for citizens)
      - Public Wi-Fi in central areas

Casablanca is working to enhance livability through improvements in urban mobility, public spaces, and digital infrastructure. The Casablanca Tramway and expanded bus networks reduce traffic congestion and provide residents with more efficient, sustainable transport options. Public and green spaces are being revitalized, including the Ain Diab Corniche for leisure and tourism and the Arab League Park for recreation and community engagement. The city is also advancing smart city initiatives, such as smart parking and waste management systems, digital city administration offering e-services to citizens, and public Wi-Fi in central areas. These measures aim to create a healthier, more connected, and environmentally sustainable urban environment for the people of Casablanca.

Innovation and Best Practices:

  • 1. Sustainable Mobility:
    • - Expansion of tramway lines connecting key districts, reducing traffic congestion and emissions.
      - Promotion of electric buses and smart traffic lights.
  • 2. Green Urban Projects:
    • - Rehabilitation of public parks and creation of eco-friendly zones like “Anfa Park.”
      - Encouragement of renewable energy in public buildings.
  • 3. Entrepreneurship & Innovation Hubs:
    • - Technopark Casablanca supports startups in ICT, renewable energy, and digital services.
      - Collaboration with universities and the private sector to foster innovation.
  • 4. Global Event Readiness (FIFA World Cup 2030 & African Cup):
    • - Casablanca is preparing major infrastructure, transport, and sustainability projects as part of hosting the African Cup and FIFA World Cup 2030.

Casablanca’s recent urban development and sustainability initiatives generate multiple benefits for residents and the environment.

  • Improved Quality of Life: Expanded tramway lines, electric buses, and pedestrian-friendly public spaces enhance mobility, reduce travel times, and promote healthier lifestyles.
  • Environmental Benefits: Rehabilitation of parks, creation of eco-friendly zones, and promotion of renewable energy reduce pollution, support biodiversity, and improve air and water quality.
  • Economic Opportunities: Technopark Casablanca and innovation hubs foster entrepreneurship, create jobs, and support local businesses in ICT, renewable energy, and digital services.
  • Cultural and Social Enrichment: Revitalized public spaces, parks, and cultural events strengthen community engagement, leisure opportunities, and civic pride.
  • Sustainable Urban Development: Smart city initiatives, including e-services, smart parking, and waste management systems, promote long-term urban resilience and environmental sustainability.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

    - Joint pilot projects on renewable energy and waste recycling systems.

  • 2. Cultural and Educational Exchange

  • 3. Entrepreneurship & Innovation

  • 4. Tourism Development

    - Exchange knowledge in urban tourism management and cultural branding.
    - With Casablanca hosting the African Cup and FIFA World Cup 2030, the city seeks partnerships in sustainable stadium design, smart transport solutions, and sports tourism promotion

  • 5. Youth & Community Engagement

    - Encourage volunteer and civic collaboration platforms.

  • Conclusion:

    - Casablanca stands as a gateway between Africa and the world, blending tradition, innovation, and sustainable progress.
    - The upcoming African Cup and FIFA World Cup 2030 preparations highlight Casablanca’s commitment to inclusive, sustainable, and global urban development.
    - The Asia–Africa City Network provides an invaluable platform to share knowledge, connect cities, and build partnerships that transcend borders.

Casablanca is a port city and commercial hub in western Morocco, fronting the Atlantic Ocean. The city's French colonial legacy is seen in its downtown Mauresque architecture, a blend of Moorish style and European art deco. Standing partly over the water, the enormous Hassan II Mosque, completed in 1993, has a 210m minaret topped with lasers directed toward Mecca.
