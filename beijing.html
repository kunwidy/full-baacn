Beijing

22.596.500 Resident

Chinese

54’39° LU 24’116° BT

Population Characteristics:

All 56 ethnic groups of China are present in this city, with 96.5% of the population being Han Chinese. The rest of the population is made up of the other 55 ethnic minorities, the biggest being Manchu, Hui and Mongol.

There are many religions in this huge city. In theory, people are free to practice any religion in the city. This is not always the case and there is some tension between different groups.

People are attracted to Beijing because of job opportunities and the better living conditions compared to rural areas. There are 7 million migrants in Beijing, making up 35% of the population. This number is continually growing and migrant workers contribute much to the city’s economy. However, social inequalities exist between them and the rest of the population.

City's Unique Identity:

Beijing’s cultural icons reflect the city’s deep imperial heritage and rich traditional life. The Forbidden City, once the grand imperial palace of the Ming and Qing dynasties, now serves as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the world’s largest museums, preserving millions of historical and artistic artifacts. The Temple of Heaven, a majestic ceremonial complex where emperors prayed for good harvests, symbolizes the ancient belief in harmony between heaven and earth. Traditional hutong neighborhoods add another layer of cultural significance, offering an authentic glimpse into local life through narrow alleys lined with courtyard houses, artisan shops, and traditional foods. Complementing these is the National Museum of China, which houses extensive collections spanning prehistoric civilizations to modern Chinese history, reinforcing Beijing’s role as a cultural and historical center.

Beijing’s landmarks represent both monumental history and iconic urban scenery. The Great Wall of China, particularly its Mutianyu and Badaling sections, stands as one of the world’s most remarkable engineering achievements and a symbol of ancient defense architecture. Tiananmen Square, one of the largest public squares globally, is a focal point of national history and is surrounded by significant structures such as the Gate of Heavenly Peace and the Mausoleum of Mao Zedong. The Summer Palace, with its harmonious blend of lakes, gardens, and palatial architecture, offers a serene retreat once enjoyed by emperors. Meanwhile, Jingshan Park, located just north of the Forbidden City, provides one of the best panoramic views of Beijing from the Wanchun Pavilion, highlighting both the city’s historic grandeur and its contemporary skyline.

  • Spring Festival (Chinese New Year): China's most important festival, celebrated for 15 days with fireworks, lion dances, and family dinners. In Beijing, the festival is celebrated with great fanfare, featuring cultural performances at venues like the Ditan Temple Fair.
  • Dragon Boat Festival: Celebrated on the fifth day of the fifth month of the lunar calendar with dragon boat races, eating bakcang (zongzi), and activities in honor of the poet Qu Yuan.
  • Mid-Autumn Festival: One of the four major festivals in China, celebrated by gathering with family, viewing the full moon, lighting lanterns, and eating mooncakes (yuebing).
  • Qingming Festival : A day to honor ancestors by visiting graves, cleaning them, and performing homage rituals.

Beijing's culinary scene is very diverse, including iconic Peking duck , various types of dumplings and noodles such as Jajangmian (black soy sauce noodles), spicy dry hotpot dishes, to street snacks such as satay and Jian Bing (typical Beijing crepe).

The main language in Beijing is Mandarin Chinese. This language originated in Beijing and is now the official language of China.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Beijing, formerly known as Peking, is the capital of the People’s Republic of China, and the country’s political and cultural center. As the second-largest first-tier city in terms of GDP, Beijing is home to the headquarters of most of China’s largest state-owned companies and houses the largest number of Fortune Global 500 companies in the country.

The city is largely a post-industrial economy and has the biggest tertiary industry as a proportion of GDP among all Chinese cities. In 2021, Beijing’s tertiary industry, or service sector, accounted for over 80 percent of the city’s GDP, while the secondary industry, or manufacturing sector, accounted for most of the remaining 23 percent.

A key component of China’s fundamental national strategy of opening up to the outside world is luring international investment. Foreign-invested businesses have made distinctive and significant contributions to the steady and quick economic growth of China and Beijing is no exception. Since 2019, the Beijing Municipal Commerce Bureau has been publishing a ‘Beijing Foreign Investment Development Report’ on an annual basis for the benefit of international investors.

City Development Focus:

Beijing’s cultural tourism continues to thrive. In 2024, the city welcomed a record-breaking 372 million visitors and tourism revenue surged to 672.24 billion RMB (equivalent to $94 billion dollars). Events like the “Jingcai New Year Lantern Carnival” alone drew 190,000 tourists and generated over 15.7 million RMB, highlighting the city’s growing cultural tourism.

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): The city is building industrial districts such as Beijing E-Town to become AI cities, encouraging research and application of artificial intelligence technologies.
  • Clean Energy Transition: The city is transitioning from coal to cleaner energy sources such as natural gas and renewable energy, and is promoting the widespread adoption of electric vehicles (EVs).
  • Integrated Transportation System: Integration of information and communication technology is used to manage traffic efficiently, including digital navigation systems and AI-based traffic lights.

  • Green Economy & Innovation Hub: Beijing is actively positioning itself as a global green economy hub, targeting major growth in green-tech innovation, strengthened green infrastructure, and deeper regional/international cooperation by 2035.
  • Improved Air Quality & Public Health: According to the Beijing Municipal Ecology and Environment Bureau (cited by People’s Daily), the city’s ecological index has improved significantly (from 64.2 to 71.4), PM2.5 levels have dropped, and the number of “good air quality” days has increased.
  • Low-Carbon Development & Climate Resilience: Beijing’s “green blueprint” emphasizes a low-carbon, climate-resilient development path: phasing out coal, promoting clean energy (gas, renewables), expanding green buildings, and strengthening carbon-finance mechanisms.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Beijing and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Since Beijing is becoming a green-economy hub, there is strong potential for technology exchange with Bandung, especially in clean energy, green building, and low-carbon transport. Bandung’s growing innovation ecosystem (tech startups, urban planning research) could benefit from Beijing’s experience in scaling green infrastructure.

Beijing’s climate adaptation plans (e.g., flood control, “sponge city” infrastructure, energy transition) can be shared with other KAA cities (including Bandung) through city diplomacy platforms. These collaborations can help cities in Asia and Africa facing climate stress to learn from Beijing’s policies and pilot projects.

Beijing’s behavioral incentives for low-carbon mobility (e.g., public transport, EVs, carpooling) can be a model for Bandung and other KAA cities seeking to reduce congestion and emissions. Shared digital platforms or joint research on transport demand management could be mutually beneficial.

Beijing’s green finance mechanisms (carbon trading, funding for green buildings) could be leveraged in cooperation with Bandung to co-develop green development projects. Together, cities can jointly apply for international green funding (climate funds, multilateral agencies) to support low-carbon/eco-urban initiatives.

Through cultural diplomacy, Beijing and Bandung can collaborate on heritage, tourism, and city branding. For instance, shared Asia–Africa city networks (or UNESCO-related city partnerships) can organize programs on sustainable tourism, green heritage conservation, or cultural exchange.
