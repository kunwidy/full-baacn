Khartoum

6,754,180 Resident

Sudan

15.5974° N, 32.5356° E

Population Characteristics:

Khartoum has a young, diverse, and rapidly growing population shaped by internal migration, economic opportunity, and urbanization. The city reflects Sudan’s national ethnic composition, with a majority of Sudanese Arabs alongside significant communities of Fur, Beja, Nuba, and other African ethnic groups contributing to its multicultural social landscape. Sudanese Arabic is the primary language spoken in daily life, while English is used in administration, education, and business, giving the city a multilingual character.

Islam is the dominant religion, primarily Sunni, forming the core of the city’s cultural and social identity, while Christian and other minority communities also contribute to Khartoum’s diversity. The population is predominantly youthful, with many residents moving from rural areas and surrounding regions to pursue employment, education, and entrepreneurial opportunities. This combination of youth, cultural diversity, and social mobility shapes Khartoum into a dynamic, modern, and socially vibrant urban center.

City's Unique Identity:

Khartoum blends Sudan’s rich traditional heritage with a developing modern cultural scene. The city celebrates iconic arts such as Sudanese music and dance, including haqiba and nubian rhythms, alongside contemporary painting, sculpture, and crafts. Cultural institutions like the National Museum of Sudan, Omdurman Cultural Centre, and local art galleries showcase the country’s archaeological treasures, handicrafts, and performing arts, highlighting both Sudan’s cultural identity and Khartoum’s emerging cosmopolitan character.

Several notable landmarks in Khartoum highlight its historical, political, and cultural significance. These include the National Museum of Sudan, which preserves the country’s archaeological and cultural heritage, and the Presidential Palace, representing the seat of national governance. The Confluence of the Blue and White Nile is a natural landmark and symbolic heart of the city. Religious landmarks include the Sayed al-Shohada Mosque and All Saints Cathedral, reflecting Khartoum’s Islamic and Christian heritage. Additionally, the Omdurman Market and Tuti Island showcase the city’s vibrant commercial and social life. Together, these landmarks define Khartoum’s identity as a city of governance, culture, and community.

Khartoum hosts a variety of cultural and artistic festivals that celebrate Sudan’s heritage and contemporary creativity. Key events include the Khartoum International Film Festival, showcasing national and international cinema, and the Khartoum Book Fair, promoting literature, education, and intellectual exchange. Traditional music and dance festivals highlight Sudanese folk traditions, including Nubian and Arab cultural performances. These festivals foster cultural exchange, support local artists, and strengthen community engagement, reflecting Khartoum’s vibrant and diverse cultural landscape

Khartoum’s culinary scene reflects Sudan’s rich and diverse gastronomic heritage. Traditional dishes include fuul medames, a fava bean stew often eaten for breakfast, and kisra, a thin fermented bread served with stews or sauces. Meat dishes like tagine-style stews and grilled meats are common, often flavored with local spices. Street food staples include sambusas (savory pastries), falafel, and bamia (okra stew). The city also offers a growing selection of international cuisine in cafés and restaurants, blending traditional Sudanese flavors with modern culinary trends. This combination makes Khartoum a vibrant center for local and regional gastronomy.

In Khartoum, Sudanese Arabic is the primary language spoken in daily life, markets, and informal communication. English is widely used in government, education, and business, reflecting Sudan’s official bilingual framework. Other languages spoken by minority communities include Nubian, Ta Bedawie, Fur, and various Nilotic and Nilo-Hamitic languages. This multilingual environment contributes to Khartoum’s rich cultural diversity and facilitates both local identity and international engagement.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Khartoum serves as Sudan’s administrative, political, and economic center, playing a central role in national development. The city hosts the national government, key ministries, the presidential palace, and major institutions for finance, industry, and trade, making it the primary hub for governance, economic activity, and public administration. Khartoum also supports education, research, and cultural initiatives, contributing to Sudan’s social and economic modernization.

Regionally, Khartoum occupies a strategic position at the confluence of the Blue and White Nile, linking northern and southern Sudan and facilitating trade and transport networks across the country. Its location and infrastructure make it a key center for regional commerce, connectivity, and coordination, contributing to national stability, development, and Sudan’s engagement with neighboring countries and broader regional frameworks in East and North Africa.

City Development Focus:

Khartoum is working to enhance livability through improvements in urban mobility, public spaces, and essential services. Initiatives include the expansion and modernization of road networks to reduce traffic congestion, the development of public parks and recreational areas, and programs to improve access to clean water and sanitation. The city is also exploring digital solutions for municipal services, including e-governance platforms and public information systems. These measures aim to create a healthier, cleaner, and more sustainable urban environment for residents while supporting Khartoum’s growth as a modern and resilient capital.

Khartoum promotes innovative and community-focused policies that strengthen sustainable urban development. The city implements key initiatives to expand access to clean and reliable water through projects improving water supply and sanitation services. Khartoum also advances programs to enhance waste management, reduce environmental degradation, and improve public hygiene, often in collaboration with national agencies, municipal authorities, and development organizations. By working with civil society, private sector actors, and international partners, the city supports initiatives that foster cleaner neighborhoods, public health, and long-term urban resilience, contributing to a more sustainable and livable capital for its residents.

Khartoum’s recent initiatives in water supply, sanitation, waste management, and urban planning generate multiple benefits for residents and the environment.

  • Improved Quality of Life: Better access to clean water and upgraded sanitation services enhance public health and daily living conditions
  • Environmental Benefits: Waste management programs and efforts to reduce environmental degradation improve air and water quality and support urban sustainability.
  • Economic Opportunities: Infrastructure projects create jobs and support local enterprises involved in water, sanitation, and environmental services.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Khartoum and Bandung have strong potential to collaborate in several areas, leveraging real initiatives and documented programs in Khartoum.

Khartoum and Bandung can exchange expertise on water supply and sanitation systems, as Khartoum is currently developing its water supply operations through JICA-supported projects. They can also collaborate on waste management, drawing on Khartoum’s solid waste collection initiatives, including the “fixed time, fixed place” system and fleet expansion. In addition, Khartoum’s Smart Sudan platform provides experience in IoT infrastructure, digital public services, and renewable energy solutions.

The cities can undertake joint pilot programs in recycling and community-driven environmental projects. Khartoum has organized recycling festivals to encourage citizens to turn waste into art, promoting both awareness and creative engagement. Bandung and Khartoum can collaborate on educational programs and campaigns to improve public sanitation and community participation in environmental management.

Collaboration could include safe water and hygiene initiatives, building on UNICEF-supported projects in Khartoum that provide water treatment for millions of residents. Joint workshops and knowledge exchanges on environmental health, water safety, and sanitation management would further strengthen public health outcomes.
