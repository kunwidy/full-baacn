Hamamatsu

799.295 Resident

Japan

34°42′39″N 137°43′39″E

Population Characteristics:

The residents of Hamamatsu have unique social characteristics shaped by a blend of innovative industrial culture and openness to cultural diversity, supported by a large expatriate population, especially Brazilians, drawn by the city’s manufacturing sector.

This multicultural environment fosters a welcoming and inclusive social atmosphere, reflected in various cultural festivals such as the Indonesian Festival. Hamamatsu’s identity is also rooted in its strong tradition of innovation and craftsmanship, being home to global manufacturers like Yamaha and Suzuki, which shapes the community’s work ethic and connection to music and automotive industries. Local residents are known for their warmth and hospitality, creating a comfortable environment for both locals and expatriate families. The city’s rich natural surroundings, its ocean, rivers, mountains, and Lake Hamana, also influence social life by promoting outdoor recreation and family-oriented activities that make Hamamatsu an attractive and family-friendly place to live.
Industry in Hamamatsu: Automotive manufacturing, musical instruments, and optoelectronics are the major industries in Hamamatsu.

City's Unique Identity:

Ikon Budaya:The primary cultural icon of Hamamatsu is its identity as a "City of Music" and innovation, home to world-class musical instrument and vehicle manufacturers like Yamaha, Suzuki, and Kawai. This legacy of craftsmanship and technological excellence defines the city's modern culture. The Hamamatsu Museum of Musical Instruments stands as a key cultural symbol, being the only large-scale public museum of its kind in Japan, displaying around 1,500 instruments from across the globe and embodying the city's deep connection to music. Another significant cultural icon is the historical association with Tokugawa Ieyasu, the revered shogun who unified Japan; he spent his formative years at Hamamatsu Castle, lending the city the nickname "Castle of Success". The local specialty, unagi (freshwater eel) from Lake Hamana, is also a culinary icon, representing the region's rich natural resources and traditional industries.

Hamamatsu features a mix of historical and modern landmarks that draw visitors. The most prominent historical landmark is Hamamatsu Castle (浜松城), a reconstruction of the fortress where Tokugawa Ieyasu lived for 17 years. The castle park features a museum, a beautiful Japanese garden, and an observatory offering panoramic views of the city. A distinctive modern landmark dominating the skyline is the Act Tower (アクトタワー), a skyscraper designed to resemble a giant harmonica, symbolizing the city's musical heritage. Nature provides its own iconic landmarks, including Lake Hamana (浜名湖), a large coastal lake known for eel farming and recreational activities, and the vast Nakatajima Sand Dunes (中田島砂丘), one of Japan's largest sand dune areas, which offers stunning natural scenery along the Pacific coast.

The main festival in Hamamatsu is the Hamamatsu Matsuri, which is held every May 3-5 during the Golden Week period.This festival is famous for its two main events: a giant kite display (Takoage) competing on the beach during the day, and a lively parade of floats at night. In addition, there are other festivals, such as the "Indonesia Festival," which celebrates the friendship between Indonesia and Japan in Hamamatsu.

Typical culinary delights of Hamamatsu City, Japan,very identical with unagi (freshwater eel) which is served in various forms, especially unagi pie (eel flavored cookies) and various other eel dishes. In addition, you can also enjoy gyoza (Japanese dumplings) and high-quality tea

The local language spoken in Hamamatsu City, Japanese is Japanese languageJapanese is the official and primary language in Japan, including in Hamamatsu, located in Shizuoka Prefecture.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

National Role

  • Automotive Industry Center: Hamamatsu is the birthplace of the founders of major automotive companies in Japan, as well as being an important production base for vehicles such as cars and motorcycles
  • Musical Instrument Production Center: The city is known as a center for musical instrument production, including pianos and keyboards, and considers itself Japan's "music city."
  • Technology and Innovation Base: The presence of automotive and musical instrument industries makes Hamamatsu a center of technology and innovation that contributes to the national economy.

Regional Role

  • Music Culture Center: Hamamatsu stands out as a center of music culture in Japan, with the Musical Instrument Museum showcasing a unique collection and opportunities to try out various musical instruments firsthand.
  • Food Destination: As a “hidden food paradise,” Hamamatsu attracts tourists with its delicious local dishes like gyoza and unagi, as well as delicious tea
  • Easily Accessible Tourist Destination: Its strategic location in Shizuoka Prefecture, about 90 minutes from Osaka by bullet train and within easy reach of Tokyo, makes Hamamatsu a popular weekend destination for both domestic and international tourists.

City Development Focus:

Tourism Focus and Main Attractions

  • Center of Industry and Innovation :
    Hamamatsu is known as the birthplace of global companies such as Yamaha and Suzuki. Tourism capitalizes on this heritage through
    • Hamamatsu Musical Instrument Museum, where visitors can enjoy music and learn the history of musical instruments.
    • Experience related to motorcycles and manufacturing.
  • Samurai History :
    The city has a rich history, including Kastil Hamamatsu, where Japan's first Shogun, Tokugawa Ieyasu, spent his youth, and Ryotanji Temple.
  • Culinary Specialties :
    Hamamatsu is promoted as a "hidden culinary paradise" with specialties such as:
    • -Locally farmed unagi (freshwater eel)
    • -Local gyoza (dumplings)
    • Delicious local tea
  • Nature Tourism and Recreation :
    The natural beauty around Lake Hamana offers activities such as clam digging, marine sports, and enjoying the views of seasonal flowers.

  • Hamamatsu has the highest sunlight exposure in Japan, making it suitable for solar energy
  • The city supports the installation of household solar panels (with a subsidy of 20,000 yen)
  • It has advanced waste-to-resource recycling technology
  • Crane operations are performed manually during the day and automatically at night to improve efficiency
  • Traffic is analyzed in real time to predict and reduce congestion
  • This is expected to improve transportation efficiency and reduce CO₂ emissions
  • The city aims to achieve carbon neutrality (net-zero CO₂ emissions) by 2050
  • It promotes the use of renewable energy, the realization of a hydrogen-based society, and sustainable innovation

  • Intercultural Integration: Hamamatsu actively promotes an "intercultural city" vision, where diverse foreign residents are considered important partners in city planning. This approach aims to create a new regional culture by leveraging diversity and fostering a community where anyone can play an active role.
  • Enhanced Quality of Life (QoL): The city focuses on improving QoL through collaborative efforts between regional, corporate, and inter-organizational entities. This includes providing necessary information and services (e.g., healthcare, social welfare, education) to all residents, including foreign residents, at various life stages, often utilizing digital tools for better access.
  • Disaster Preparedness and Safety: Initiatives include using digitalization to share crisis information rapidly and conducting disaster preparedness drills, raising disaster awareness among all residents.
  • Economic Opportunities and Job Creation: Hamamatsu's strong industrial base, home to global companies like Yamaha and Suzuki, has historically provided significant employment opportunities and supports local economic growth
  • Community Building: Activities such as local cleaning campaigns and supporting residents' associations help foster social cohesion and a sense of community pride and shared responsibility.
  • Educational Support: The city provides educational and career assistance for children and young people with foreign roots, promoting equal opportunities and social inclusion.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Hamamatsu and Bandung have been sister cities since 2014, collaborating in the fields of culture, education, and tourism. Hamamatsu is known for its International Piano Competition, while Bandung is famous as a city of arts and creativity, making their partnership a bridge that strengthens friendship and mutual understanding. Their cooperation includes educational nature-conservation tours in Tangkuban Perahu and Lake Hamana, the exchange of ideas on managing tourism-related waste, and Green Tourism activities such as joint tree-planting programs. The relationship between Hamamatsu and Bandung highlights the importance of intercity cooperation across Asia and Africa. Such collaboration serves as a bridge for culture, education, and innovation, driven not only by governments but also by communities, students, and young artists. Together, Bandung and Hamamatsu can become a model of creative cities that integrate technology and culture. Joint activities, such as cultural festivals, student exchanges, and environmental projects, continue to strengthen solidarity. This city network is expected to become a future platform for young people across Asia and Africa to learn and innovate together.

Hamamatsu is a coastal city in Honshu, Japan's main island. The Nakatajima Sand Dunes, a breeding ground for loggerhead turtles, fringe the coastline. Near the Act Tower skyscraper, designed to resemble a harmonica, are the global exhibits of the Hamamatsu Museum of Musical Instruments. The reconstructed 16th-century Hamamatsu Castle has more than 400 cherry trees and an observatory with Pacific Ocean views.
