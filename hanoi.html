Hanoi

8.808.000 Resident

Vietnam

20°25' ° N 105°30' ° E

Population Characteristics:

Vietnam in general and Hanoi, in particular, has undergone the influences of different social standards and values of Confucianism, Communism and some major religions as Buddhism, Catholicism, and also experienced a tremendous social change in recent years.

As a result, moral education in Hanoi nowadays stands for many forms and definitions. Despite the fact that Hanoi people are still pure like they used to be, they become more and more outgoing, more subtle than before, they are positive to the integration as well as the overseas friends.

City's Unique Identity:

The cultural icons of Hanoi represent the city's enduring traditions, historical values, and the spirit of its people, often transcending physical structures to embody national identity. The Khue Van Cac (Pavilion of the Constellation of Literature), located within the Temple of Literature complex, is the official symbol of Hanoi, representing the nation's historical dedication to education, literature, and intellectual pursuit. The lotus flower is another significant cultural icon, symbolizing purity, resilience, and enlightenment as it rises untainted from muddy water, reflecting the Vietnamese spirit. Traditional elements like the Ao Dai (traditional long dress), the Non La (conical hat), and even iconic local cuisine such as Pho also serve as powerful, everyday cultural icons that define Vietnamese heritage and daily life in Hanoi.

Hanoi's landmarks are prominent, physical sites that stand as testaments to its rich history and architectural heritage. Hoan Kiem Lake (Lake of the Returned Sword) is arguably the city's most central and symbolic landmark, steeped in legend and serving as a major gathering place for locals and tourists alike. The lake features the picturesque Turtle Tower on a small islet and the striking red-painted The Huc Bridge leading to the Ngoc Son Temple. Other notable landmarks include the solemn Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum complex, the ancient One Pillar Pagoda known for its unique lotus-on-water architecture, the historic Imperial Citadel of Thang Long, and the imposing French-colonial Hanoi Opera House. The bustling Hanoi Old Quarter, with its narrow streets and traditional artisan shops, is itself a living landmark that preserves the layout and lifestyle of ancient Hanoi.

  • Tet Nguyen Dan (Tahun Baru Vietnam): Festival terbesar tahunan yang dirayakan bersamaan dengan Tahun Baru Imlek, biasanya pada bulan Februari. Perayaan ini termasuk pesta jalanan, parade, dan kembang api, serta reuni keluarga dan tradisi Tahun Baru.
  • Festival Gióng: Festival tradisional yang diadakan untuk menghormati pahlawan mitologi Thánh Gióng.
  • Festival "Ao Dai" Wisata Hanoi: Festival tahunan yang diadakan pada bulan November yang bertujuan memuliakan Ao Dai (busana tradisional Vietnam) dan mempromosikan citra Hanoi sebagai ibu kota budaya dan kreativitas.
  • Festival Pagoda Parfum: Festival ziarah keagamaan yang diadakan di Pagoda Parfum (Chua Huong), berlangsung dari awal Tahun Baru Imlek hingga akhir Maret dalam kalender lunar.
  • Festival Pertengahan Musim Gugur: Festival yang dirayakan pada bulan kesembilan kalender lunar dan melibatkan lentera, tarian barongsai, serta makanan tradisional.

Hanoi's culinary scene is very diverse, including classic dishes such asphở (noodle soup) and bún chả (grilled pork with rice noodles), popular street foods such as bánh mì (Vietnamese sandwiches) and bánh cuốn (rice rolls), and unique drinks such as egg coffee (coffee with egg foam). These foods can be enjoyed from breakfast to dinner.

The main local language in Hanoi isVietnamese​, with a specific dialect known as North Vietnamese . This is the official language of the country and the mother tongue of most of the city's population. English is widely spoken in the tourism sector, and French may also be spoken among older generations due to the former colonial history.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

  • Center of Government and Politics : As the capital of Vietnam, Hanoi is the administrative center, home to the headquarters of ministries, government agencies, and 78 foreign embassies. The city leads the way in implementing the country's development policies and guidelines.
  • Heart of Culture and History : Hanoi is a repository of Vietnam's cultural and historical heritage, home to thousands of heritage sites and intangible cultural heritage sites, such as Hoan Kiem Lake and the Old City. The city strives to preserve and develop its national cultural identity.
  • Education and Human Resources Center : The city is a major education center, housing the Vietnam National University system and investing in the development of quality human resources and innovation.
  • Engine of Economic Growth : Hanoi serves as the core and convergence center of economic development in the Red River Delta region and the entire country, attracting foreign investment and spearheading regional development movements.

City Development Focus:

The global booming demand for tourism has created significant economic opportunities for travel destinations in Southeast Asia. The international tourism demand is projected to grow by roughly 4 percent per annum over the next decade. Southeast Asian destinations have been key beneficiaries of this demand, receiving 130 million international visitors in 2018 (9.3 percent of total global international visitor flows). The region’s share of global demand is also expected to expand further to 10.4 percent by 2030 (187 million visitors).

Vietnam, with its vibrant natural and cultural tourism assets, is expected to benefit from the strong market demand. According to the World Economic Forum’s (WEF) 2017 Tourism Competitiveness Index, Vietnam ranks 32nd globally (out of 120 countries) in terms of the volume and attractiveness of natural and cultural resources, and 3rd within Southeast Asia. Moreover, Vietnam is home to 8 UNESCO World Heritage sites, tied with Indonesia at the top spot for SEA. The country also has premier urban tourism destinations such as Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, and Da Nang, which have been growing rapidly for the past years as well.

Over the past decade, Vietnam has experienced a boom in both inbound and domestic tourism. The number of international tourist arrivals has reached nearly 14.5 million in the first 10 months of 2019 (13% more than the same period last year). Domestic tourism, which is greater in volume than inbound tourism from abroad, has experienced excellent growth as well. In 2018, there was a four-fold increase in the number of domestic traveler trips, from 20.5 million in 2008 to 80 million in 2018, manifested by Vietnam’s rapidly-growing middle class, who have a strong appetite for travel and leisure.

Vietnam’s strong inbound tourism growth has enabled it to capture a growing share of tourism demand in the region. Over both the past 5-10 years, Vietnam’s international visitor growth has consistently outranked that of its competitors in Southeast Asia. With this, Vietnam has been gradually capturing greater market share, not only of total tourism arrivals to developing Southeast Asia, but also of those to the broader East Asian region, including more developed tourism markets such as Japan, Singapore, China, and South Korea.

  • High-Quality Human Resources: Hanoi considers high-quality human resources (HR) as the "golden key" to a new era of development integrating technology, science, innovation, and culture. This initiative aims to make Hanoi a hub for innovation and digital transformation in Vietnam.
  • Sustainable Tourism (Green Tourism): The Tourism Authority of Thailand (TAT) in Bangkok is leading the future of tourism with a strong focus on sustainability, innovation, and digital transformation . This "green tourism" initiative emphasizes a balance between environmental, cultural, and economic benefits for local communities.
  • Public Transportation Improvements: To meet the surge in tourists and keep the city running smoothly, Bangkok is investing in improving and expanding its public transportation system, such as extending the railway line (purple line) connecting major tourist attractions.
  • Soft Power Diplomacy: Thailand uses tourism as part of its soft power diplomacy, promoting not only tourist attractions but also national identity and cultural values ​​through local cuisine and participation in international events.

  • Hanoi is undertaking a major push toward sustainable architecture and green infrastructure as part of its 2021–2030 master plan. The city aims to expand public green space (parks, lakes, pedestrian areas) and integrate digital tools (GIS, IoT) to monitor air, water, and energy quality.
  • To address frequent flooding, planners are advocating for a “sponge city” model: upgrading drainage, restoring natural water retention zones such as lakes and canals, and using green-blue infrastructure (e.g., rain gardens, permeable surfaces) to buffer heavy rainfall.
  • Hanoi is revitalizing its many lakes (known as its “green lungs”), such as West Lake and Hoan Kiem, which provide ecological services, improve city microclimates, and offer recreational and community spaces.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Hanoi and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Adopting the sponge-city approach. Bandung can learn from Hanoi’s efforts to integrate lakes and blue-green infrastructure into urban flood management.

Bandung could partner with Hanoi on green building initiatives, sharing technical knowledge about vertical gardens, green roofs, and passive cooling design. KAA cities might jointly propose city-to-city training or demonstration projects on sustainable architecture, leveraging Hanoi’s emerging standards.

Bandung and Hanoi could share their experiences in deploying IoT / GIS for environmental data (air, water, energy), enabling better planning and real-time urban management. In a broader Asia-Africa network, these cities could co-develop open-source tools or dashboards for climate-resilience metrics, funded by multilateral climate programs.
