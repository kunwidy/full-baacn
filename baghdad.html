Baghdad

8.141.120 Resident

Iraq

33.33° LU, 44.4° BT

Population Characteristics:

The social characteristics of the current population of Baghdad are characterized by ethnic and religious diversity, with the majority of the population being Arab Muslims divided into two main sects, namely Shia and Sunni. The majority of the population is Muslim Arab.

However, the city also has minority communities of Christians, Yazidis, Mandaeans, and others, reflecting the region's long history of multiculturalism. The division between Shia and Sunni Muslims is a significant aspect of Baghdad's social and political dynamics. Sectarian tensions occasionally arise, affecting social interaction and the city's stability. Historically, Baghdad was known as a cosmopolitan metropolis that attracted a wave of literary enthusiasts and scholars from diverse cultural backgrounds, including Persia, Ancient Greece, India, and Syria. This multiculturalism has enriched local art, literature, and traditions.

City's Unique Identity:

  • Al-Mutanabbi Street : This street is a vibrant cultural center and a major icon of Baghdad's intellectual life.Known for its numerous bookshops and outdoor bookstalls, it has been a gathering place for writers, students, and intellectuals for generations, with a history dating back to the Abbasid era. The street embodies the city's enduring legacy as hub for learning and literature, a reputation solidified by the presence of the iconic statue of the famous 10th-century Arab poet, Al-Mutanabbi, after whom the street is named . The nearby Shabandar Café , a historic coffeehouse, further adds to the area's iconic status as a place of cultural exchange and discussion.
  • The National Museum of Iraq : While physically a building, its collection represents the very essence of Iraq's cultural identity, housing a wealth of art and artifacts from Mesopotamian,Sumerian, Babylonian, and Assyrian civilizations. The museum's collections,although heavily looted following the 2003 invasion, remains a symbol of Iraq's globally important historical legacy as the "cradle of civilization". It serves as a poignant cultural icon representing both a glorious past and the tragic impact of conflict on national heritage.

  • Al-Shaheed Monument (Martyr's Memorial) : This is one of Baghdad's most prominent modern landmarks, a massive split dome structure inaugurated in 1983 to commemorate the casualties of theIran-Iraq War. Standing approximately 150 feet (50 meters)high, the monument is a powerful symbol of national sacrifice and pride. Its distinctive blue-tiled architecture makes it a visually striking and emotionally significant public art piece, easily recognizable in the city's skyline.
  • The Victory Arch (Swords of Qadisiyah) : Also known as the "Hands of Victory"or "Crossed Swords," this landmark consists of two enormous arches, each featuring a pair of forearms and hands holding crossed swords. Erected in 1989on a parade ground, the monument was built to celebrate Iraq's self-proclaimed victory in the Iran-IraqWar and symbolizes the nation's military strength and resilience.
  • Al-Kadhimiya Mosque and Shrine: Located in the Kadhimiya suburb, this mosque is a vital religious landmark, significant to Shia Muslims as it integrates the shrines of two important imams. Characterized by its stunning golden domes and intricate decorative elements, it is an active pilgrimage site that attracts millions of visitors annually. The architectural splendor and religious importance of the site make it a key physical and spiritual landmark of Baghdad.

  • Baghdad International Theatre Festival : This annual event (despite being interrupted due to conflict) serves as an important platform for the global theatre community to encounter Iraqi culture, showcasing a variety of performances from across the country.
  • Baghdad International Flower Festival : This is an annual event featuring floral and horticultural exhibitions in downtown Baghdad.
  • International Festival for Iraqi Children : This festival is held at the Baghdad International Fair and is dedicated to children.
  • Crafts and Folklore Festival : This festival showcases a collection of paintings, handicrafts, traditional clothing, and sculptures that reflect the richness of Iraq's ancient heritage.
  • Babylon International Festival : Although usually held in the ancient city of Babylon, this international music and arts festival has returned after a decades-long absence, signaling a broader Iraqi cultural renaissance.
  • Religious Celebrations : As a Muslim-majority country, major religious festivals such as Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr are important celebrations in Baghdad and throughout Iraq. Shia religious observances such as Ashura are also celebrated, though often in other holy cities in Iraq.
  • Spring Festival of Culture, Language & Culinary : This event has also been held, supported by entities such as the Indonesian Embassy in Baghdad.

Culinary delights in Baghdad include Iraqi specialties such as masgouf (grilled fish), tashreeb (bread and meat soup), and quzi (grilled lamb or chicken with rice). Other dishes include dolma (stuffed vegetables), falafel (chickpea fritters), and kofta (meatballs). Visitors can also find international dishes like pizza and burgers.

The main local language in Baghdad isBaghdad Arabic, a dialect of Arabic that serves as the lingua franca in Iraq and is widely used in commerce and education. Furthermore, Kurdish is recognized as an official language in Iraq and is spoken by a portion of the population, particularly in the northern region.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

  • Capital and Center of Government : Baghdad is the capital of the Republic of Iraq and serves as the country's governmental, administrative, and political center. All major government institutions, ministries, and the presidential palace are located here.
  • Economic and Trade Center : As Iraq's largest city, Baghdad is a major center of economic activity. Strategically located on the banks of the Tigris River, the city serves as a vital domestic trade hub, despite facing challenges from the ongoing conflict.
  • Cultural and Educational Center : Baghdad has a rich cultural life, with numerous museums, universities (such as the University of Baghdad), and educational institutions. The city is an intellectual and artistic center for the Iraqi nation.

City Development Focus:

The development of tourism in Baghdad is experiencing significant positive momentum, particularly driven by the city's designation as the "Arab Tourism Capital" for 2025.Despite still facing post-conflict challenges, the Iraqi government is actively working to revitalize the sector with a focus on cultural heritage, history, and religious tourism.

Baghdad's designation as the Arab Tourism Capital 2025 by the Arab Tourism Organization is a recognition of its rich history and the government's efforts to promote stability and improve tourism infrastructure. Religious tourism is a major driver of tourism growth throughout Iraq, including Baghdad. Tens of millions of pilgrims visit the country's holy sites each year, contributing to economic recovery.

  • Nissor Square Project : This is a major multi-level transportation hub project that includes the construction of elevated roads, tunnels, and the renovation of underground pipelines and cables. The project has significantly reduced traffic congestion and reduced noise pollution in the capital.
  • "Development Road" (Iraq-Europe Development Road) : Iraq is launching this ambitious infrastructure project worth trillions of Rupiah that aims to connect West Asia with Europe through a network of railways, highways, ports and new cities, making Baghdad a key trade crossroads again.
  • Bismayah New City Project (BNCP) : This is a new city development initiative located 10 kilometers southeast of Baghdad, which includes the construction of 108,000 modern housing units in an area of ​​18.3 square kilometers.

Baghdad plays an important role in Iraq’s social recovery and cultural revival: UNESCO and UN agencies have supported heritage conservation, training, and cultural-institution recovery that restore civic identity and create jobs in conservation and museum sectors. The city is also benefitting from national reconstruction and development initiatives, backed by the World Bank and multilateral partners, that aim to rebuild public infrastructure, improve basic services, and reintegrate conflict-affected communities into the economy.

Environmental and planning research highlights growing efforts to protect and restore urban green spaces and heritage landscapes in and around Baghdad (which help with air quality, urban cooling, and community recreation), while recent studies on urban heat, waste management, and environmental management show an increasing policy focus on monitoring, green interventions, and environmental standards. Finally, UNDP programming and international reconstruction projects provide technical assistance and capacity building that strengthen municipal governance, disaster risk reduction, and community resilience, contributing measurable social and environmental benefits during recovery.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Baghdad has clear, practical entry points for city-to-city cooperation with Bandung and other KAA cities in areas where Bandung already has strengths (creative economy, urban planning, smart governance) and where Baghdad needs capacity and partners:

Baghdad’s UNESCO-supported heritage work and the regional training centre history make heritage conservation exchanges (restoration techniques, craft-skills training, museum management) a natural match with Bandung’s cultural and creative networks. Joint training programs, short fellowships for conservators, and co-developed heritage-tourism products would be high-impact.

World Bank reconstruction frameworks and local studies point to needs in flood control, heat mitigation, and green-space revitalization. Bandung–Baghdad pilots could adapt Bandung’s urban-green/park solutions and Bandung’s research capacity (ITB) to Baghdad’s context (and vice versa on arid-zone water management lessons). Such pilots would be strong candidates for multilateral funding.

Baghdad is exploring smart-city enablement and digital governance; Bandung’s recent experience in smart city roadmaps, data platforms, and public-private innovation ecosystems could support peer learning (technical assistance, platform design, and joint workshops). City-to-city technical exchanges could accelerate Baghdad’s digital service rollout.
