Ankara

5,550,490 Resident

Turkey

39°55′44″N 32°51′17″E

Population Characteristics:

Ankara, the capital of Turkey, has a young and growing population shaped by significant internal migration, economic opportunity, and its role as the political and administrative center of the country. The city is predominantly Turkish, reflecting the national demographic composition, with minority communities including Kurds, Circassians, and other groups contributing to its social diversity.

Turkish is the main language spoken in daily life, government, and education, while minority languages such as Kurdish are also present within the city’s communities. Religiously, Ankara is primarily Muslim (Sunni), though the city also includes other religious communities, reflecting Turkey’s broader social landscape. Ankara’s population is generally youthful, mobile, and increasingly educated, making the city a dynamic, modern, and socially vibrant capital.

City's Unique Identity:

Ankara blends Turkey’s rich historical heritage with a growing contemporary cultural scene. The city celebrates traditional arts such as Turkish folk music, Anatolian dances, and calligraphy, alongside modern visual arts, theatre, and literature. Cultural institutions including the Ankara State Opera and Ballet, CerModern Contemporary Art Center, and Museum of Anatolian Civilizations showcase the country’s artistic traditions and historical legacy. These institutions and artistic activities highlight both Turkey’s national cultural identity and Ankara’s emerging cosmopolitan character.

Several notable landmarks in Ankara reflect its political significance, historical heritage, and cultural richness. These include the Anıtkabir, the mausoleum of Mustafa Kemal Atatürk and a symbol of modern Turkey, and the Museum of Anatolian Civilizations, which preserves artifacts from Turkey’s ancient civilizations.

Other key sites include Kocatepe Mosque, one of the largest mosques in the country, the Presidential Complex (Cumhurbaşkanlığı Külliyesi) representing the seat of national governance, and Atakule Tower, offering panoramic views of the city.

Together, these landmarks define Ankara’s identity as a city of history, governance, and cultural significance.

Ankara hosts a variety of cultural and national festivals that celebrate Turkey’s heritage, arts, and community life. Key events include the International Ankara Music Festival, showcasing classical and contemporary music performances, and Ankara International Film Festival, highlighting national and international cinema.

Other celebrations include National Sovereignty and Children’s Day and Republic Day, marked by parades, ceremonies, and public events.

These festivals reflect Ankara’s commitment to cultural preservation, artistic expression, and community engagement.

Ankara offers a rich culinary scene that blends traditional Turkish flavors with modern influences. Signature dishes include Ankara tava (a lamb and rice specialty), çibörek (deep-fried pastry with meat filling), and kofta.

The city is also known for its vibrant street food culture, including simit (sesame-coated bread rings), kebabs, and local desserts like baklava and lokum.

Restaurants, cafes, and markets across Ankara showcase both authentic Anatolian cuisine and contemporary culinary innovations, reflecting the city’s dynamic cultural character.

The primary language spoken in Ankara is Turkish, used in daily life, education, government, and business.

Minority languages such as Kurdish, Circassian, and Arabic are also present within certain communities, reflecting the city’s cultural diversity.

English is commonly used in higher education, international business, and tourism, giving Ankara a multilingual urban character.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Ankara serves as the administrative and political heart of Turkey, hosting the national government, ministries, and key state institutions. The city was purposefully developed to support governance, with areas such as Kızılay and Çankaya accommodating government offices, public institutions, and diplomatic missions. Ankara also plays a central role in education, research, and policy-making, contributing to national planning, public services, and socio-economic development.

Regionally, Ankara occupies a strategic position in central Anatolia, linking major industrial, agricultural, and transport hubs across the country. The city’s road, rail, and air networks connect central Turkey to other regions, facilitating trade, logistics, and mobility. Through its administrative, economic, and infrastructural functions, Ankara contributes significantly to national stability, development, and regional integration within Turkey.

City Development Focus:

Ankara offers diverse tourism experiences that combine history, culture, and modern urban life.

  • Preservation and Promotion of Cultural Sites. The city emphasizes the conservation of landmarks such as Anıtkabir, the Museum of Anatolian Civilizations, and Kocatepe Mosque, providing educational and cultural experiences for visitors.
  • Development of Arts and Cultural Communities. Ankara supports festivals, exhibitions, and arts programs that showcase Turkish music, crafts, and performance arts. Cultural centers like CerModern and local galleries offer opportunities for visitors to engage with contemporary and traditional art.
  • Nature and Recreation Tourism. Surrounded by green spaces such as Gençlik Park and Atatürk Forest Farm, the city provides outdoor recreation, walking paths, and eco-friendly leisure areas.
  • Improving Accessibility and Tourism Infrastructure. Tourist accessibility is enhanced through well-connected public transport, including buses, metro lines, and easy access to Ankara Esenboğa Airport. Public facilities are continuously upgraded to improve convenience and visitor experience.
  • Cultural Diplomacy and International Exchange. Ankara participates in international collaborations through cultural festivals, exhibitions, and academic programs, promoting the city as a hub for cultural dialogue and regional significance.

Ankara implements innovative and sustainable urban policies to enhance the city’s livability, efficiency, and resilience. The city has introduced smart street lighting systems that include LED lights, air quality sensors, security cameras, Wi‑Fi access, and charging stations for electric vehicles and wheelchairs.

Renewable energy is promoted through initiatives like the solar power installation at the AŞTİ bus terminal, reducing reliance on conventional electricity. The city’s metro and light rail systems are incorporating greener technologies such as regenerative braking to lower CO₂ emissions.

Ankara also focuses on waste management and eco-friendly urban planning, with central monitoring for energy efficiency and solid-waste operations. These measures, along with support for green spaces and biodiversity, demonstrate Ankara’s commitment to a modern, sustainable, and smart urban environment that balances technology, culture, and environmental stewardship.

Ankara’s innovative urban policies and sustainable initiatives deliver multiple benefits for residents and the environment.

  • Improved Quality of Life: Smart street systems, efficient public transport, and green spaces enhance mobility, safety, and recreational opportunities, promoting healthier and more active lifestyles.
  • Environmental Benefits: Renewable energy use, regenerative braking in rail transit, and waste management reforms reduce emissions, air pollution, and environmental degradation.
  • Economic Opportunities: Sustainable infrastructure projects, smart city technologies, and renewable energy initiatives create jobs, stimulate local businesses, and support entrepreneurship.
  • Cultural and Social Enrichment: Investments in public spaces, cultural hubs, and inclusive urban planning strengthen community engagement and civic pride.
  • Sustainable Urban Development: Energy-efficient buildings, smart monitoring systems, and eco-friendly urban design promote a resilient, environmentally conscious, and future-ready city.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Ankara and Bandung have meaningful potential to collaborate in sustainable urban development, innovation, and governance, grounded in documented initiatives from Ankara’s municipal and strategic plans:

Ankara’s “Smart Ankara” master plan includes a Sustainable Urban Mobility Plan (SUMP) and an Intelligent Transport System (ITS) vision.

Both cities could partner on smart street lighting: Ankara is deploying smart lighting fixtures equipped with LED, EV/USB charging, Wi-Fi, and security cameras.

Shared projects on renewable energy are possible: Ankara’s AŞTİ bus terminal rooftop solar plant is producing roughly half of its own power.

Ankara is expanding its metro system, having recently broken ground on the Mamak Metro line.

The city’s strategic plan includes adding more bike lanes and integrating rail transport to reduce car dependency.

Bandung and Ankara could exchange knowledge on regenerative braking and energy‑efficient rail systems, building on Ankara’s existing rail infrastructure.

Ankara is listed in the Smart Cities Turkey network, supporting IoT integration, digital public services, and efficient infrastructure management.

Academic research from Ankara shows implementation of ICT-based smart city governance, energy efficiency, and integrated digital services.

The city’s 2025–2029 Strategic Plan emphasizes sustainable transport, citizen-oriented services, and environmental goals, showing a commitment to inclusive governance.

Joint capacity-building programs can be designed, such as workshops on smart city planning, participatory budgeting, or digital governance, allowing Bandung and Ankara to learn from each other’s experiences.

Ankara, Turkey’s cosmopolitan capital, sits in the country’s central Anatolia region. It’s a center for the performing arts, home to the State Opera and Ballet, the Presidential Symphony Orchestra and several national theater companies. Overlooking the city is Anitkabir, the enormous hilltop mausoleum of Kemal Atatürk, modern Turkey’s first president, who declared Ankara the capital in 1923.
