Riyadh

7.953.000 Resident

Saudi Arabia

38’24° LU 43’46° BT

Population Characteristics:

Life in Riyadh is concentrated around the city’s more than 18,000 mosques and its numerous busy shopping centers. The city’s central core and its many souks (marketplaces) attract heavy pedestrian traffic, emphasizing the city’s intense feeling of vitality.

As residents of a major city in a mainly Muslim (see Islam) country, Riyadh’s inhabitants adhere to a number of social norms that include segregation of the sexes and the need to protect the privacy of the family. Recreational activities are often a family affair, and large public gatherings are mainly restricted to male attendants. However, many activity centers allow women and families to attend at special times or in reserved areas.

City's Unique Identity:

Riyadh’s cultural identity is defined by a blend of historical heritage and traditional urban life. At its core is Diriyah, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the birthplace of the Saudi state, showcasing Najdi architecture and the city’s early political legacy. Complementing this is the National Museum of Saudi Arabia, which presents the kingdom’s history, Islamic civilization, and regional cultural development through modern, immersive galleries. Masmak Fortress stands as a symbol of the nation’s unification, while Al Rajhi Mosque represents the grandeur of contemporary Islamic architecture and serves as a major religious hub. Traditional cultural life is reflected in Souq Al Zal, one of the city’s oldest markets offering handicrafts, spices, and traditional goods. Additionally, Burj Al Khazzan, originally a historical water tower, has been re-envisioned as the “Tree of Life,” a cultural and ecological landmark symbolizing Riyadh’s commitment to sustainability and regeneration.

Riyadh’s skyline is marked by modern architectural icons that express the city’s rapid transformation. The Kingdom Centre Tower, one of the most recognizable structures in Saudi Arabia, features a distinctive Sky Bridge providing panoramic views of the metropolitan landscape. Al Faisaliyah Tower, another major architectural symbol, is known for its unique tapered design crowned by a glass sphere. The city also features the King Abdullah Mosque, integrated within the Kingdom Centre Tower, which stands as one of the tallest and most visually striking mosques in the country. Beyond its modern architecture, Riyadh offers family-oriented attractions such as the Riyadh Zoo, a popular recreational destination that contributes to the city’s urban leisure offerings.

Main Festival:

  • Riyadh Season: A multi-sectoral festival aimed at promoting tourism, culture, and entertainment, featuring music concerts, art performances, and sporting events. Designed for all ages, the festival attracts visitors from around the world.
  • Janadriyah Festival: A national cultural and heritage festival, it is one of the most important cultural events in the Middle East. It showcases Saudi Arabia's rich culture, history, and arts, and serves as a platform for many countries to showcase their cultures, such as the Indonesian Pavilion at the 2018 festival.

Seasonal and other special festivals:

  • Riyadh Spring Festival: A festival organized by the Riyadh Municipality that highlights spring with massive landscape decorations involving the planting of millions of flowers and ornamental plants.
  • King Abdulaziz Falconry Festival: The festival, which focuses on the tradition of falconry, attracts falconers and hunting enthusiasts from Saudi Arabia and the GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council) countries to participate in competitions such as al-Mallouah and Mazayen.

Culinary in Riyadh includes traditional Saudi Arabian dishes such as Kabsa Rice and Harees , popular street foods such as Mutabak , as well as a selection of international and fine dining dishes.Some notable spots include Najd Village for authenticity and Tatel on Bujairi Terrace for fine Spanish cuisine.

The main local language in Riyadh isNajdi dialect, a distinctive dialect of Najdi Arabic that is also part of the Gulf (Khaliji) Arabic dialects. In addition to local dialects, English is also widely understood, especially in urban and business areas.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Riyadh has been the political capital of Saudi Arabia since the unification of the kingdom in 1932. The city is the headquarters of the King, the royal palace, and all the ministries and central government agencies. All the country's major political and administrative decisions are made here.

Riyadh is the economic engine of Saudi Arabia, contributing nearly 50% of the country's non-oil GDP. It is a major commercial, financial, and manufacturing center.

Riyadh is at the forefront of the Saudi Vision 2030 economic modernization initiative, which aims to diversify the economy away from oil dependence. Major projects, such as the King Abdullah Financial District and the Green Riyadh program, are part of this effort. The city serves as a major transportation and education hub in the kingdom, with many international schools and universities to serve the large expatriate and local population

City Development Focus:

Riyadh’s tourism sector has expanded rapidly in recent years, driven by major heritage and mega-projects as well as national Vision 2030 reforms that aim to diversify the economy and scale up visitor numbers. The Diriyah Gate development and other cultural investments have repositioned Riyadh as a heritage and cultural destination while large entertainment projects (and planned openings such as Qiddiya) are broadening leisure capacity and international appeal. National statistics show this momentum: Saudi Arabia recorded record tourism volumes in 2023–2024 and the government reports more than 100 million total visitors in recent years, underlining strong domestic and inbound growth that benefits Riyadh’s hospitality and services sectors.

Economically, global industry analyses highlight substantial gains in travel-related spending and jobs across the Kingdom, signaling measurable macroeconomic impacts for Riyadh. At the community level, empirical research, including the study by Alsaloum, Romagosa and Alotaibi, shows residents weigh both benefits (employment, infrastructure, cultural promotion) and costs (congestion, social change), and that their knowledge, attachment, and involvement strongly influence local support for further tourism development. Overall, Riyadh’s tourism trajectory is characterized by fast growth, large public-private investments in culture and entertainment, and an increasing need to balance economic gains with community well-being and sustainable planning.

Riyadh city, under the umbrellaSaudi Vision 2030 has several innovations and superior policies that focus on sustainable development, transformation into a smart city, and cultural development.

  • Application of AI and IoT: The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI), the Internet of Things (IoT), and big data to optimize traffic management, energy use, and urban service delivery.
  • The "Green Riyadh" project: This major initiative aims to plant 7.5 million trees across the city, increase green space per capita, and use recycled water for irrigation, which is expected to reduce the city's temperature and improve air quality.
  • Riyadh Metro: A large-scale modern public transportation project that aims to modernize transportation in Saudi Arabia, reduce congestion and environmental impact.

Riyadh’s rapid transformation under Saudi Vision 2030, backed by large public investments and the Kingdom’s tourism strategy, has generated significant social and economic benefits: growth in tourism-related employment, expanded cultural venues, and new leisure and heritage attractions that create jobs and diversify local livelihoods. Flagship heritage projects such as Diriyah Gate (managed by the Diriyah Gate Development Authority) preserve UNESCO-listed cultural assets while deliberately integrating community programs, skills training, and local business opportunities, which support heritage-based livelihoods and cultural education. On the environmental side, Riyadh’s city programs and recent municipal projects (including large environment, water, and agriculture investments) aim to expand green cover, strengthen water and sanitation infrastructure, and pilot sustainable urban projects, improving urban resilience, public health outcomes, and liveability for residents.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Riyadh and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Bandung’s experience with community cultural programs and creative-economy initiatives could partner with Diriyah’s conservation teams to share skills training, craft/value-chain development, and visitor-management practices.

Riyadh’s investments in water, green infrastructure, and resilience projects can be paired with Bandung’s knowledge in urban planning, participatory design, and research partnerships led by ITB.

Bandung’s active role convening Asia–Africa city networks provides a ready institutional channel (symposia, working groups, and donor-facing consortia) to include Riyadh and other KAA cities in thematic programs on heritage, tourism, and climate.
