Dakar

3,658,640 Resident

Sinegal

14°41′34″N 17°26′48″W

Population Characteristics:

Dakar is a vibrant, youthful, and multicultural capital city, shaped by migration, education, and economic opportunity. Its population mirrors Senegal’s broader ethnic makeup: Wolof is the largest group, followed by Pular (Peul), Serer, Mandinka, Jola, and Soninke.

In terms of language, French serves as the official language, while Wolof is widely spoken in daily life. Other local languages such as Pular, Serer, Jola, Mandinka, and Soninke also have strong presence.

The city’s religious landscape is predominantly Muslim, mostly affiliated with Sufi brotherhoods, while a smaller portion of the population identifies as Christian, primarily Roman Catholic. Dakar’s population is very young: across Senegal more than 60% of the population is under 25 years old. This youthful demographic contributes to the city’s dynamic social life, strong economic potential, and continuous demand for education, infrastructure, and innovation.

City's Unique Identity:

Dakar blends Senegal’s rich cultural heritage with a growing contemporary arts scene. The city celebrates traditional music such as Mbalax, along with storytelling, dance, and visual arts. Contemporary art, literature, and film also thrive, with institutions like the Village des Arts, IFAN Museum of African Arts, and the Dakar Biennale (Dak’Art) showcasing both national traditions and innovative artistic expressions. These cultural icons highlight Dakar’s dynamic identity and emerging cosmopolitan character.

Dakar is home to several notable landmarks that reflect its historical, cultural, and political significance. Key sites include Gorée Island, a UNESCO World Heritage site and symbol of the Atlantic slave trade, the African Renaissance Monument, representing national pride and modern identity, the Presidential Palace, serving as the seat of government, and IFAN Museum of African Arts, preserving Senegalese and African artistic heritage. These landmarks collectively define Dakar as a city of history, governance, and cultural prominence.

Dakar hosts a variety of festivals that celebrate its vibrant cultural life and artistic creativity. These include the Dakar Biennale (Dak’Art), a major contemporary African art festival, and the Saint-Louis Jazz Festival, attracting international musicians and audiences. Traditional and religious festivals, such as Tabaski (Eid al-Adha) also play a significant role in the city’s social calendar. These events highlight Dakar’s commitment to preserving cultural heritage while fostering community engagement and international cultural exchange.

Dakar offers a rich culinary scene that reflects Senegal’s diverse cultural heritage and coastal resources. Popular dishes include thieboudienne (fish and rice), yassa poulet (marinated chicken with onions and lemon), and mafe (peanut-based stew). Street foods such as fataya (fried pastries) and accara (black-eyed pea fritters) are widely enjoyed. Seafood is abundant, given Dakar’s coastal location, and local markets provide fresh ingredients for traditional and modern cuisine. The city’s culinary offerings highlight both local traditions and its evolving urban culture.

In Dakar, Wolof is the most widely spoken language and serves as the primary means of daily communication. French is the official language, used in administration, education, and business. Other local languages such as Pular, Serer, Jola, Mandinka, and Soninke are also spoken by various communities, reflecting Dakar’s linguistic diversity and multicultural identity.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Dakar is the economic, political, and cultural heart of Senegal. Economically, it is a dominant force the Dakar region alone accounts for nearly half of the country’s GDP. The city’s port is one of the most important in West Africa, serving as a key logistics and trade hub for exports and imports.

On the national level, Dakar concentrates a large portion of Senegal’s formal employment: it hosts a high share of permanent jobs in commerce, transport, industry, and banking. Administratively, the region plays a major role in planning, land use, and development.

Regionally, Dakar is recognized as a key diplomatic and economic gateway in West Africa. It hosts important international institutions, summits, and diplomatic events, strengthening its role in ECOWAS and continental cooperation. The city is also investing in transport infrastructure, including a regional express train (TER) and a major highway, to improve connectivity with other parts of Senegal and neighboring countries.

City Development Focus:

Dakar is working to enhance urban livability through initiatives focused on sustainable mobility, green spaces, and inclusive public services. The city has improved public transport with bus and regional train systems to reduce congestion and pollution. Efforts are underway to expand public parks and recreational areas, providing citizens with spaces for leisure and community activities. Urban planning initiatives emphasize environmental sustainability, accessibility, and social inclusion, aiming to create a healthier, safer, and more comfortable environment for all residents. These measures contribute to making Dakar a more liveable and resilient city.

Dakar is driving several groundbreaking and sustainable urban initiatives to improve mobility, reduce emissions, and create a greener, more inclusive city:

  • Electric BRT & Sustainable Public Transport : Dakar recently launched a fully electric Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) system with around 120–144 articulated e-buses running on clean energy. The World Bank is supporting the city’s transformation with a US$ 100 million program to integrate BRT, regional express rail (TER), and restructured bus networks, while professionalizing informal transport operators.
  • Low‑Carbon Fuel Innovation : Through a partnership with IFC and CETUD, Dakar is building a biomethane production plant that converts organic waste into biofuel to power public buses — reducing reliance on fossil fuels.
  • Smart Traffic Management : As part of its PPPs, Dakar is introducing a dynamic traffic signal system with advanced controllers and radar to optimize flow, improve safety, and reduce congestion.
  • Transit‑Oriented Urban Development : A major project is underway in the Petersen neighborhood around the BRT terminal, planning mixed-use development with energy-efficient buildings, green spaces, and soft mobility (walking, cycling).
  • Sustainable Mobility Strategy : The Executive Council for Sustainable Urban Transport (CETUD) has a long-term Urban Mobility Plan (PMUD) up to 2035 that prioritizes multimodal transport, reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, and low-carbon public transport.

Dakar’s urban innovations and sustainable policies bring multiple benefits to residents and the environment:
  • Improved Quality of Life: Expanded electric BRT and regional rail networks reduce traffic congestion, enhance mobility, and make daily commuting safer and more comfortable.
  • Environmental Benefits: The shift to electric buses, biomethane fuel, and green urban planning reduces greenhouse gas emissions, lowers air pollution, and promotes cleaner public spaces.
  • Economic Opportunities: Transit-oriented development, infrastructure projects, and professionalization of transport operators generate jobs and stimulate local entrepreneurship.
  • Social Inclusion: Improved public transport access and pedestrian-friendly areas benefit women, youth, and marginalized communities, promoting equity and civic participation.
  • Sustainable Urban Growth: Integrated planning for mobility, energy, and urban development fosters long-term resilience, climate adaptation, and a citizen-centered city.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Dakar and Bandung have strong potential for cooperation across sustainable mobility, urban development, innovation, and social programs, anchored in Dakar’s recent and officially documented initiatives:

  • 1. Sustainable Urban Mobility
    • Bandung and Dakar can partner on electric public transport systems, drawing from Dakar’s all-electric BRT network of 121 articulated e-buses.
    • Both cities could collaborate on transit-oriented development (TOD) around major transport hubs. Dakar’s Petersen neighborhood project, for example, integrates green neighborhood development with walking and cycling infrastructure.
    • Bandung could benefit from Dakar’s experience with dynamic traffic signal management, which uses advanced controllers and radar to optimize flow in a climate-resilient transport system.
  • 2. Green Energy & Low-carbon Transport
    • Joint initiatives on biofuel and waste-to-energy conversion: Dakar is developing a biomethane plant that turns organic waste into bus fuel, a model for Bandung’s circular economy ambitions.
    • Knowledge sharing on financing and scaling renewable energy in public transit, based on Dakar’s PPPs supported by IFC and international partners.
  • 3. Urban Planning & Public Space
    • Collaborative research and projects on sustainable neighborhood design, leveraging Dakar’s transit-oriented development vision to plan mixed-use, green, and walkable areas.
    • Integration of cycling and pedestrian infrastructure: Dakar’s 2035 Sustainable Urban Mobility Plan (PMUD) targets 175 km of cycling lanes. Bandung, which also aims to improve non-motorized transport, can exchange expertise and best practices.
  • 4. Innovation & Public‑Private Partnerships
    • Bandung’s innovation ecosystem can collaborate with transport PPP models from Dakar, such as the financing and operation of sustainable transport solutions structured by IFC and CETUD.
    • Joint capacity-building on urban transport governance: Bandung can learn from CETUD’s institutional framework, which coordinates sustainable transport policy and PPPs.
  • 5. Social & Economic Inclusion
    • Collaborate on mobility equity programs, focusing on making public transport more accessible for low-income and vulnerable populations, as Dakar’s BRT and PMUD emphasize inclusive mobility.
    • Exchange programs to support green jobs: both cities can develop training for young people in green transport, electric mobility, and sustainable urban planning, building on Dakar’s job creation through its BRT PPPs.

Dakar is the capital of Senegal, in West Africa. It’s an Atlantic port on the Cap-Vert peninsula. Its traditional Médina quarter is home to the Grande Mosquée, marked by a towering minaret. The Musée Théodore Monod displays cultural artifacts including clothing, drums, carvings and tools. The city’s vibrant nightlife is inspired by the local mbalax music.
