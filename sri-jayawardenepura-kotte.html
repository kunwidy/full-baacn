Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte

2.234.289 Resident

Sri Lanka

6° 54' LU, 79° 54' BT

Population Characteristics:

The social characteristics of the population of Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte, the legislative capital of Sri Lanka, closely reflect the general demographics of the country, which is dominated by certain ethnic and religious groups.

The majority of the population is Sinhalese , who are the largest ethnic group in Sri Lanka as a whole (about 75% of the national population). Significant minority groups include Tamils ​​and Muslims. The primary religion practiced is Buddhism , in line with the national majority of Sri Lanka. Other minority religious groups include Hinduism (especially among ethnic Tamils), Islam, and Christianity. As the administrative capital, Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte is the center of government activities, and has the social characteristics of an urban society, with diverse economic and social levels. Despite being dominated by one ethnic group, the city remains a meeting point for the various cultures, traditions and festivals of the various ethnic and religious groups in Sri Lanka.

City's Unique Identity:

Cultural icons refer more to the values, traditions and sites that represent the historical and spiritual identity of a city, which is often closely linked to the heritage of the ancient Kingdom of Kotte.

  • Heritage of the Ancient Kotte Kingdom : The city's primary cultural identity stems from its role as the capital of the Sinhalese Kingdom of Kotte ( 15th–16th centuries). The archaeological remains from this period, while not as grand as those of other ancient cities, are important cultural icons, representing a golden era in Sri Lankan history.
  • Theravada Buddhism : Buddhism is a central aspect of the city's culture. Ancient Buddhist temples such as the Kotte Raja Maha Vihara (Great Vihara of the Kotte Kingdom) serve as spiritual and cultural centers. Dating back to 1415 , the temple is renowned for its frescoes and peaceful atmosphere, and hosts an annual traditional dance, known as the Perahera.
  • Sacred Tooth Relic : Historically, Kotte was home to the original Temple of the Sacred Tooth Relic of the Buddha (Dalada Maligawa), a three-story structure dating back to the royal era. Although the relic is now in Kandy, its historical significance remains a key part of Kotte's cultural iconography.
  • Traditional Dances : Traditional dance performances, as seen during the annual temple processions, are living cultural icons that showcase Sri Lanka's artistic heritage.

A city landmark is a prominent and easily recognizable geographical or architectural feature, whether historic or modern.

  • Sri Lanka's Parliament Complex : This is the city's most prominent and modern landmark. Designed by renowned architect Geoffrey Bawa, the building sits on an artificial island in the middle of Lake Diyawanna Oya, showcasing magnificent architecture reminiscent of ancient Sri Lankan palaces. The surrounding area also serves as a popular recreational park.
  • Diyawanna Oya Lake : This beautiful artificial lake surrounds the parliament building and is an important urban natural landmark. The area is popular for boating, bird watching, and evening strolls along the food court.
  • Beddagana Wetland Park : An important ecological area and recreational landmark, offering opportunities to observe wildlife and enjoy nature within the city limits.
  • Kotte Museum : An archaeological museum showcasing stone, metal, and pottery artifacts from the ancient Kotte Kingdom, it serves as an important educational landmark for visitors wishing to understand the city's history.
  • Kotte Ambalama : A historic roadside rest house dating from the Kotte period (15th century), used as a resting place for visitors to the ancient capital. It is a preserved historical landmark.

Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte, the administrative capital of Sri Lanka, celebrates a variety of national and religious festivals, similar to those celebrated throughout the country. Some of the major festivals present or relevant in the city include:

  • Sinhalese & Tamil New Year (April) : This is the country's most important cultural festival, celebrated by the Sinhalese and Tamil communities, marking the end of the harvest season and the beginning of the new year. The celebrations involve family traditions and special dishes.
  • Vesak Poya (May) : The most important Buddhist festival, commemorating the birth, enlightenment, and death of Gautama Buddha. Cities, including Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte and Colombo (the adjacent commercial city), are decorated with colorful lanterns (called Vesak kudu ) and pandals (themed displays), with many charitable and religious activities.
  • Poson Poya (June) : Celebrates the arrival of Buddhism in Sri Lanka. Pilgrimages are often made to Anuradhapura, but religious celebrations also take place in the capital.
  • Independence Day (February 4) : A national holiday celebrated with patriotic ceremonies, parades, and national pride.
  • Deepavali / Diwali (October/November) : The Hindu Festival of Lights widely celebrated by the Tamil Hindu community, involving lighting of oil lamps, fireworks and prayers at temples.
  • Navam Perahera (February) : Although primarily held at the Gangaramaya Temple in Colombo, this grand parade (perahera) features traditional dancers, drummers, and decorated elephants, and is a major cultural celebration in the capital region.
  • Duruthu Perahera (January) : Held at Kelaniya Raja Maha Viharaya, an ancient Buddhist temple near Kotte, this festival is another important pageant that attracts large crowds.

  • Rice and Curry: A staple dish consisting of white rice or steamed rice served with fish or meat curry, as well as various vegetable, lentil, or fruit curries, accompanied by pickles and sambol.
  • Kottu: A popular street food, made from finely chopped roti prata sautéed with vegetables, egg or meat, and curry spices.
  • Pittu: A dish made from cylindrical steamed rice mixed with grated coconut.
  • Hoppers (Appa): A type of crispy, bowl-shaped pancake, often served with an egg in the center (egg hoppers) and enjoyed for breakfast or dinner.

The main local language or official language spoken in Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte, which is the administrative capital of Sri Lanka, is Sinhala and Tamil.

In addition to these two official languages, English is also widely used for official, commercial, and educational purposes. Most residents, especially in urban areas such as Kotte and Colombo (the adjacent commercial capital), are fluent in one or both of these official languages, and many also speak English.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

  • Administrative and legislative capital : Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte has been designated as the administrative capital and the seat of the Parliament of Sri Lanka since its inauguration in 1982.
  • Solution to traffic congestion : The relocation of the administrative center to Kotte is an effort to address the problem of traffic congestion and density in Colombo, which remains the commercial capital.
  • Planned government center : The city is designed as a modern urban area, with government offices located on Diyawanna Oya Island.
  • Higher education hub : The renowned Sri Jayewardenepura University is also located in the city, adding to its role as a national hub of education and science.

City Development Focus:

Post-Crisis Revival: Sri Lanka's tourism industry, and by extension Kotte, has shown spectacular growth recently. In early July 2025, the country recorded a surge in tourist arrivals, signaling a strong recovery in the tourism sector.

Focus on Sustainable Tourism: There is a strong push to promote sustainable tourism and responsible practices. This involves environmental conservation, local community development, and cultural preservation.

Infrastructure and Connectivity Development: The Sri Lankan government has launched ambitious tourism plans and new projects to improve connectivity. One recent initiative (August 2025) is a feasibility study to connect Neduntheevu and Katchatheevu Islands, which is expected to boost tourism growth in the region.

  • Integrated Urban Planning: This development plan adopts a strategic approach to balance economic, social and environmental growth, with the aim of making the city the "Main Gateway of the Administrative Capital of Sri Lanka".
  • Sustainable and Environmentally Sound Development: The city is targeting climate-smart and sustainable urban development, with a particular focus on the management of the wetland area around Diyawanna Oya, which is recognized as a Ramsar Convention wetland site.
  • "Clean Sri Lanka" Program: The city government allocates significant efforts and funding for city cleanliness and beautification initiatives under this national program, encouraging citizen participation in maintaining a clean and disease-free environment.
  • Parliament Infrastructure on Reclaimed Land: One of the unique features of the city is the construction of the new parliament complex on a reclaimed island in the middle of an artificial lake (Diyawanna Oya), which demonstrates innovation in engineering and spatial planning for administrative purposes.

Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte (the administrative capital area within Greater Colombo) has shown measurable positive impacts through planned urban management, wetland protection, and capacity-building programs that benefit residents and the local environment. The municipal and regional development plans explicitly protect wetlands and promote integrated land-use to prevent harmful encroachment and dumping, which helps preserve ecosystem services (flood buffering, groundwater recharge) for local communities.

Major infrastructure and sector projects (sewerage/master-planning, metro/urban regeneration) supported by multilateral partners aim to reduce environmental health risks (improved sanitation, reduced pollution), create local jobs during construction and operation, and improve public amenities, thereby raising local quality of life. Environmental and social management frameworks are in place to mitigate negative effects and to boost inclusive benefits.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Bandung and SJK can exchange practice on protecting urban wetlands, floodplain management, and nature-based drainage solutions (e.g., “sponge-city” type approaches adapted to local climates). SJK’s legal zoning for wetland protection and Colombo region planning provide concrete entry points for technical exchange and joint pilot demonstrations.

Ongoing sewerage and metro-area projects in SJK (with JICA/World Bank involvement) create opportunities for Bandung to share experience on decentralized sanitation, service provision models, and community engagement, useful for peer learning and co-funded technical assistance.

SJK’s role as a pilot in GEF/UNEP and national sustainable-cities programs means it is already eligible for international funding and knowledge networks. Bandung’s active Asia–Africa city diplomacy (and tech/innovation ecosystem) can help assemble joint proposals (heritage + resilience + livelihoods) targeting Cities Alliance, GEF, UNESCO, or bilateral donors.
