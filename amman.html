Amman

2,273,240 Resident

Jordan

31°56′59″N 35°55′58″E

Population Characteristics:

Amman, the capital city of Jordan, is a dynamic urban center shaped by migration, economic opportunity, and regional developments. The city hosts a diverse population that reflects Jordan’s broader demographic composition, including a majority of ethnic Jordanians alongside significant communities of Palestinians, Circassians, Chechens, and other minority groups. Arabic is the primary language spoken in daily life, while English is widely used in business, education, and government, giving the city a multilingual character.

Islam is the dominant religion, primarily Sunni, forming the core of Amman’s cultural and social identity. Christian and other minority communities also contribute to the city’s diverse religious landscape. The population is relatively young and increasingly urbanized, with many residents moving from rural areas and neighboring countries in search of employment, education, and economic opportunity. This combination of youth, cultural diversity, and social mobility shapes Amman into a vibrant, modern, and socially dynamic capital.

City's Unique Identity:

Amman blends Jordan’s rich historical heritage with a growing modern cultural scene. The city celebrates traditional arts such as Jordanian folk music, dabke dance, and Bedouin crafts, alongside contemporary painting, sculpture, and performing arts. Cultural institutions and galleries, including the Jordan National Gallery of Fine Arts and the Royal Cultural Center, showcase the country’s artistic traditions, highlighting both Jordan’s cultural identity and Amman’s emerging cosmopolitan character.

Amman features several notable landmarks that reflect its historical depth and modern development. The Citadel (Jabal al-Qal’a) offers archaeological insights and panoramic city views. The Roman Theatre, a well-preserved structure from the 2nd century AD, hosts cultural events and performances. King Abdullah I Mosque stands as a prominent symbol of religious and architectural significance. Modern civic landmarks, such as Abdali Boulevard and The Boulevard Amman, showcase contemporary urban design and commercial life. Together, these sites highlight Amman’s blend of history, culture, and modernity.

Amman hosts a variety of cultural and artistic festivals that celebrate Jordan’s heritage and contemporary creativity. Notable events include the Amman International Film Festival, which showcases regional and global cinema, and the Amman International Theatre Festival, highlighting performing arts from Jordan and abroad. Traditional festivals, such as Jordan National Day celebrations and local cultural fairs, feature music, dance, and crafts, reflecting the city’s commitment to preserving heritage while fostering community engagement and cultural exchange.

Amman offers a vibrant culinary scene that reflects Jordan’s traditional flavors and contemporary dining trends. Popular dishes include mansaf, the national dish made with lamb, yogurt, and rice; falafel and hummus; and grilled meats and mezze. The city also features modern cafes, international restaurants, and street food markets, creating a diverse food culture that blends local traditions with global influences, catering to both residents and visitors.

The primary language spoken in Amman is Arabic, specifically the Jordanian dialect used in daily communication. English is widely used in business, education, government, and tourism, giving the city a multilingual character and facilitating international interaction.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Amman serves as the administrative, political, and economic heart of Jordan, hosting the national government, key ministries, and foreign embassies. The city is a central hub for education, commerce, and culture, supporting universities, research institutions, and cultural centers that contribute to Jordan’s social and economic development.

Regionally, Amman occupies a strategic position in the Levant, connecting Jordan with neighboring countries such as Syria, Iraq, and Saudi Arabia. The city facilitates trade, transportation, and regional cooperation, while hosting international conferences and diplomatic initiatives that enhance Jordan’s role in Middle Eastern stability, economic growth, and cultural exchange.

City Development Focus:

Amman is positioning itself as a major heritage and cultural tourism destination, making use of its rich history, vibrant urban fabric, and modern amenities. According to the Jordan Ministry of Tourism & Antiquities, visitors to Amman can explore ancient landmarks, traditional neighborhoods, and new cultural attractions.

  • Historic and Archaeological Sites The Amman Citadel, located on one of the city’s highest hills, offers archaeological treasures such as the Umayyad Palace complex, the Temple of Hercules, and a Byzantine church. The nearby Roman Theatre, restored and still in use for cultural events, further connects tourists with the city’s Roman heritage.
  • Museums and Cultural Institutions The Jordan Museum, located in downtown Amman, presents Jordan’s cultural and historical narrative through carefully curated galleries. Other notable museums include the Jordan Archaeological Museum at the Citadel and the Folklore Museum, which celebrates traditional Jordanian crafts, costumes, and music.
  • Vibrant Cultural Streets & Public Life Rainbow Street, in Jabal Amman, is a lively destination for both locals and tourists. It features art galleries, cafes, craft shops, and cultural performances, making it ideal for walking tours and evening leisure.
  • Panoramic Public Spaces The Hashemite Plaza, near the Roman Theatre, offers scenic gathering spaces for public events, cultural performances, and relaxation.
  • Cultural Tourism Development The Jordanian government is restoring heritage structures and upgrading infrastructure to enhance the visitor experience. The “Citadel‑Theatre Trail” project aims to improve connectivity between the Citadel and the Roman Theatre, creating a pedestrianized heritage route with restored buildings, galleries, and cafés.
  • Tourism Infrastructure & Visitor Experience Amman’s tourism strategy includes developing “soft” tourism focusing on heritage, cultural engagement, and experiential travel. According to the Ministry of Tourism & Antiquities, this approach includes leveraging the city’s hills, archaeological sites, museums, and public spaces.

Amman promotes innovative and targeted urban policies to enhance cultural, environmental, and economic development. Key initiatives include:

  • Heritage Conservation and Urban Regeneration The city has launched projects to restore and preserve historic areas, including the Amman Citadel, Roman Theatre, and traditional neighborhoods. These initiatives aim to integrate cultural heritage with modern urban life, improving accessibility and visitor experience.
  • Smart City and Digital Governance Amman is implementing e-services for citizens, digital administration platforms, and smart lighting in public spaces to enhance urban efficiency, safety, and environmental sustainability.
  • Sustainable Mobility The city supports improved public transport, pedestrian-friendly streets, and traffic management systems to reduce congestion and promote cleaner transport options.
  • Tourism and Cultural Promotion Initiatives focus on developing integrated cultural tourism circuits linking key landmarks, museums, and heritage streets. Amman encourages cultural festivals, exhibitions, and community engagement programs to strengthen its position as a heritage and arts hub.
  • Youth and Creative Economy Amman fosters innovation and entrepreneurship through creative hubs, arts programs, and youth-centered initiatives, aiming to engage younger generations in cultural, economic, and technological development.

Amman’s urban and cultural initiatives provide multiple benefits for residents and the environment:

  • Improved Quality of Life: Heritage conservation, urban regeneration, and public space enhancements create more accessible, safe, and aesthetically pleasing environments for residents and visitors.
  • Environmental Benefits: Smart lighting, traffic management, and pedestrian-friendly streets reduce energy consumption, vehicle emissions, and urban pollution, contributing to a cleaner and healthier city.
  • Economic Opportunities: Tourism development, cultural festivals, and creative economy programs generate jobs, support local businesses, and stimulate entrepreneurship, particularly among youth.
  • Cultural Enrichment: Restoration of historic sites, museums, and heritage neighborhoods strengthens cultural identity, promotes civic pride, and encourages community engagement.
  • Sustainable Urban Development: Integration of digital governance, e-services, and eco-friendly mobility supports long-term resilience, efficient city management, and environmentally conscious growth.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Amman and Bandung have strong potential to partner across sustainable tourism, heritage preservation, and urban innovation, building on Amman’s current strategies and policies in Jordan:

Amman’s Ministry of Tourism is developing site‑management plans for key cultural landmarks like the Amman Citadel to improve accessibility, preserve history, and involve local communities. Bandung could collaborate with Amman on heritage-led tourism projects, sharing expertise in site rehabilitation, inclusive planning, and community participation.

Jordan is actively promoting sustainable tourism policies; the first national forum on sustainable tourism was held in Amman to discuss strategies such as reducing environmental footprints and managing natural resources. Bandung and Amman could jointly develop eco‑tourism products, green infrastructure, and conservation projects that benefit both cities.

Amman’s resilience strategy includes promoting a sense of belonging, cultural identity, and civic participation. Bandung and Amman could launch joint programs to revive cultural neighborhoods, support creative spaces, and strengthen citizen engagement around heritage and identity.

As part of its 2025 priorities, Jordan plans to digitize tourism data, develop a tourism data-management platform, and modernize its national tourism strategy. Bandung could partner in these efforts by sharing digital tourism tools, data governance models, and smart-city solutions to enhance the visitor experience and sustainable management.

Jordan has committed to involving local communities in tourism through site‑management plans, ensuring accessibility and benefits for diverse groups, including women, the elderly, and people with disabilities. Bandung and Amman could collaborate on social-impact tourism projects, training programs, and community-led tourism entrepreneurship to empower residents and preserve cultural heritage.
