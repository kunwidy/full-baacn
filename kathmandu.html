Kathmandu

1.672.900 Resident

Nepal

27°42′N 85°18′E

Population Characteristics:

City dwellers Kathmandu has unique social characteristics, which reflects a blend of ancient Hindu and Buddhist traditions with rapidly developing urban modernity.

Kathmandu is home to a multiethnic population. The indigenous inhabitants of the Kathmandu Valley are the Newars, who have a culture, rituals, and cuisine that differs significantly from other Nepalese ethnic groups, although they are generally Hindu or Buddhist. This ethnic diversity coexists harmoniously. The majority of the population is Hindu, followed by Buddhism. These two religions have coexisted for centuries, and religious and cultural celebrations are a major part of daily life in Kathmandu.

City's Unique Identity:

The cultural identity of Kathmandu is deeply intertwined with its religious practices, historical royalty, and the unique artistic traditions of the Newar people. A prominent cultural icon is the concept of the Kumari , the Living Goddess, a young girl worshiped as an incarnation of the goddess Taleju. She lives in the Kumari Ghar in Durbar Square, and her rare public appearances are highly significant cultural events. Another key cultural icon is the rich tradition of Newari architecture and craftsmanship, visible in the intricate wood carvings, pagoda-style temples, and ancient courtyards found throughout the valley. The annual Indra Jatra festival , a grand celebration featuring masked dances and the procession of the Living Goddess's chariot, is a vibrant cultural expression that embodies the city's lively spirit and religious harmony.

Kathmandu is home to several UNESCO World Heritage Sites that serve as its most important physical landmarks. Kathmandu Durbar Square is a historic palace complex that was the royal seat of the Malla and Shah kings, featuring a stunning collection of temples, courtyards, and statues, including the formidable statue of Kala Bhairava . The ancient Buddhist stupas of Swayambhunath (popularly known as the " Monkey Temple ") and Boudhanath are iconic landmarks with their large, white domes and the "all-seeing" eyes of the Buddha, serving as major pilgrimage sites and prominent physical feature of the city's skyline. Additionally, the sacred Pashupatinath Temple , a sprawling Hindu temple complex on the banks of the Bagmati River, is a vital landmark and a revered site for Hindu cremations. Other notable physical landmarks include the historic pond of Rani Pokhari and the bustling tourist hub of Thamel neighborhood.

  • Dashain: Nepal's longest and most important festival, celebrating the victory of good over evil.
  • Tihar: A five-day festival of lights, each day has unique rituals, including the worship of crows, dogs and cows, and concludes with the "Bhai Tika" event.
  • Indra Jatra: The largest and most vibrant street festival, celebrated for eight days by the Newari community in the Kathmandu Valley.
  • Gai Jatra: A festival held to commemorate deceased family members, where cows are decorated and paraded through the streets.
  • Buddha Jayanti: A Buddhist holiday celebrating the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha, usually celebrated by gathering at monasteries and stupas such as the Boudhanath Stupa in Kathmandu.
  • Chhath Parva: A festival celebrated mainly by worshiping the sun god, usually held along river banks or near ponds.
  • Teej: A unique festival centered around women, where they fast and pray for the well-being of their families.

Culinary delights in Kathmandu, Nepal, includeDal Bhat (rice with lentil soup), Momo (steamed dumplings), Gundruk (fermented green vegetables), and Sel Roti (sweet fried bread). In addition, there are also Chatamari (Nepali pizza), Thukpa (noodle soup), and various Pahari dishes originating from the mountains.

The main local language in Kathmandu is Newar language (Nepal Bhasa) , which is the native language of the inhabitants of the Kathmandu Valley , known as the Newars . However, because Nepal considers Nepali as its official language and lingua franca, Nepali is also widely understood and used there, as is English due to its role in government and business.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

  • Political and Administrative Center: Kathmandu is the capital and seat of the government of Nepal, housing most of the central government offices, international organizations, and embassies.
  • Economic Center: The city is Nepal's most important business center and is supported by strong tourism and service sectors. Furthermore, Kathmandu is also a base for small-scale manufacturing industries such as handicrafts, carpets, and apparel.
  • Transportation Hub: With the presence of Tribhuvan International Airport, Kathmandu serves as a major gateway for national and international transportation, both air and land, connecting other cities within the country and the outside world.
  • Cultural and Educational Center: Kathmandu is home to the majority of Nepal's educated workforce, as well as being a cultural and educational center with many historical monuments and UNESCO world heritage sites.

City Development Focus:

Nepal is one of the most popular travel destinations in the world. With thousands of tourists visiting the county every year, Tourism in Nepal has risen up to new heights. Nepal has become a popular tourist hotspot in the world. The capital city of the country, Kathmandu and the city of lakes, Pokhara are the main cities in the country to see a sharp increase in the number of tourists in recent years. With trekking routes to Everest and Annapurna also flooded by foreigners annually, trekking has added the boost to increase the tourism market.

From UNESCO world heritage sites to different ancient destinations, from a diverse ethnic community to experiencing different cultures and traditions, all of which have been successful to get the interest of foreigners to visit the country of Nepal. Kathmandu’s long cultural heritage and recent urban programs generate notable social and environmental benefits. The Kathmandu Valley, a UNESCO World Heritage landscape, supports livelihoods through heritage tourism and crafts while UNESCO-backed conservation management helps protect monuments and promote community stewardship of cultural assets.

Urban and national studies also show that improving basic services (solid-waste management, sanitation) and targeted climate-resilience measures reduce health risks and create green jobs: World Bank and ADB assessments of Nepal’s waste and urban systems identify ongoing reforms and investments that strengthen service delivery and local livelihoods in the Kathmandu metropolitan area. Finally, Nepal’s national climate strategies and adaptation planning (National Adaptation Plan 2021–2050) and regional resilience programming (ICIMOD, CRCAP tools) are helping municipalities like Kathmandu integrate flood, landslide and air-pollution risk management into city planning, producing long-term benefits for vulnerable communities and urban ecosystems.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Kathmandu and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Bandung’s creative-economy practice and its UNESCO Creative Cities links can exchange approaches with Kathmandu on community-based heritage tourism, skills training for artisans, and visitor-management that protects fragile monuments while supporting local incomes.

Both cities face municipal waste challenges; Kathmandu’s national/World Bank/ADB work on solid-waste systems creates scope for joint pilots (informal-sector inclusion, recycling micro-enterprises, composting) and donor proposals.

Kathmandu’s needs around flood, landslide, and heat vulnerability match Bandung’s interest in green infrastructure and urban resilience. Co-designing “sponge-city” adaptations, early-warning systems, and nature-based hillside stabilization projects would be mutually useful and attractive to multilateral funders.

ITB/Bandung universities and Kathmandu’s institutions can run short exchanges, joint research, and training modules (heritage conservation techniques, resilient building retrofits, community waste enterprises) to build local capacity and generate pilot data for scaling.
