Population Characteristics:

Almost the entire population of Yemen, including in Sana'a, is Muslim, with significant Sunni and Shia populations. Religion plays a central role in daily social and cultural life.

The population is largely ethnically Arab, and the people hold Arab customs and traditions in high regard. Yemeni culture is generally considered one of the least affected by globalization in the Arabian Peninsula, retaining many aspects of "authentic" Arab culture. Social structures in Yemen, including in Sana'a, are often still closely tied to tribal systems and family ties. Loyalty to family and tribe is often a crucial aspect of social interaction.

City's Unique Identity:

The old city is surrounded by a massive wall 20–30 feet (6–9 metres) high, pierced by numerous gates. Most notable architecturally is the Yemen Gate (Bāb al-Yaman), renamed Liberty Gate after the revolution of 1962. Old Sanaa includes 106 mosques, 12 hammams (baths), and 6,500 houses, all built before the 11th century ce. Multistoried tower houses, built of dark basalt stone and brick, are decorated with intricate frieze work and beautiful carved windows. Sanaa’s most notable mosque, Al-Jamīʿ al-Kabīr (Great Mosque), contains a sacred shrine that was once a principal object of Zaydī veneration.

The main landmarks of Sana'a city includeThe Old City of Sana'a, a UNESCO World Heritage Site , the Great Mosque of Sana'a (one of the oldest mosques in the world), and the Yemen Gate (Bab al-Yaman) which is more than 1,000 years old.. In addition, other landmarks include the Salt Market (Suq al-bihi), Dar al-Hajar, and various other historic buildings in the Old City filled with traditional tower houses.

  • Sana'a Old City : Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, this area is a labyrinth of historic ancient buildings
  • The Great Mosque of Sana'a : One of the oldest mosques in the world, built in the early Islamic period and part of a UNESCO World Heritage Site
  • Bab al-Yaman (Yemen Gate): An iconic gate over 1,000 years old that serves as the entrance to the old walled city
  • Salt Market (Suq al-bihi) : A historic market in the old city that sells a wide variety of goods, from spices to handicrafts
  • Dar al-Hajar : A stunning castle, located in Wadi Dahr, about 30 minutes from the city center
  • Al Saleh Mosque : A more modern mosque and one of the largest in Sana'a
  • Ghumdan Palace : The ruins of an ancient palace whose structure became the prototype for the city's tower houses
  • Jabal Nuqum : A mountain that surrounds the city and according to legend, is the place where the city was built after the death of Noah

Religious and National Festivals:

  • Prophet Muhammad's Birthday : This is one of the biggest celebrations in Sana'a. Hundreds of thousands of people gather in the city center, often decorated with green flags, to commemorate the Prophet Muhammad's birthday, which falls on the 12th of Rabi al-Awwal every year. This event is a national holiday in Yemen.
  • Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha : As in other Muslim-majority countries, the holidays of Eid al-Fitr (marking the end of Ramadan) and Eid al-Adha (marking the culmination of the Hajj pilgrimage) are widely celebrated in Sana'a with prayers, family gatherings, and celebrations.
  • Unity Day : This national holiday is celebrated on May 22 every year, marking the unification of North and South Yemen in 1990.
  • Revolution Commemorations : Commemorative ceremonies are also held to mark important events in Yemen's history, such as the 60th anniversary of the revolution held in Sana'a in September 2022.

Cultural and Economic Festival:

  • Yemen's Bounties Festival : This festival is held to promote local agricultural products and is usually held in Sana'a in October.
  • Shamsan Festival : This important cultural event showcases the essence of Yemeni culture through traditional dance performances such as Al-Bara (traditional sword dance) and Al-Atan (group dance accompanied by rhythmic drum beats).
  • Summer Festival : In the past, this festival used to attract international artists to Sana'a and last for a month, featuring a variety of arts and cultural activities.

The cuisine of Sana'a, Yemen, is rich in traditional dishes such asSaltah (national soup made from boiled meat), Fahsa (spicy lamb soup), Madfoon (rice and meat with lots of spices), and Aseeda (flour dough served with broth). Popular drinks are coffee and tea, including a unique drink made from coffee skin called Qishr.

The main local language in Sana'a is Yemeni Arabic dialect. Officially, the language used is Modern Standard Arabic, but in everyday conversation, local dialects are used. Yemeni dialects have regional variations, and the dialect in Sana'a (in the north) has its own peculiarities, such as the pronunciation of the letter 'q' which is pronounced like a hard 'G'.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

Sana'a has historically been the capital of Yemen and the seat of government for most of the early states in the region. Today, the city remains a political center, although control of the government is divided due to the ongoing conflict. Sana'a is the headquarters of the Houthi movement, making it the primary center of power in northern Yemen.

For centuries, Sana'a has been a major economic and trade center in the Yemeni highlands. Its strategic location makes it a crucial hub for internal trade and the distribution of goods.

Sana'a is one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities. The Old City of Sana'a, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, reflects Yemen's ancient history and rich Islamic civilization. The city was also a center for the spread of Islam in the 7th century and boasts numerous historic mosques and important cultural institutions, such as the national museum and the military museum.

City Development Focus:

The tourism industry in Yemen is relatively small compared to other countries in the region. However, the country has a rich cultural and historical heritage, as well as beautiful natural landscapes, that can attract adventurous and curious travelers

Tourism in Yemen has faced significant challenges due to the ongoing civil war and political instability in the country, as well as security concerns for travelers. The US State Department currently advises against all travel to Yemen due to terrorism, civil unrest, and the potential for armed conflict. Before the conflict, some of the main attractions in Yemen included the ancient city of Sana’a, which is a UNESCO World Heritage site known for its unique architecture and traditional markets. Other popular destinations included the historic city of Shibam, the island of Socotra, and the ancient city of Marib, which was once the capital of the Sabaean Kingdom.

Despite the challenges facing the tourism industry in Yemen, some adventure travelers and researchers still visit the country to explore its unique culture, landscapes, and history. Yemen’s government and international organizations are working to rebuild and promote the tourism industry, with a focus on sustainable and responsible tourism practices that benefit local communities.

Due to the conflict causing frequent power grid disruptions and uncertain emergency diesel fuel supplies, more than 50% of households and critical facilities, including hospitals, in Yemen (including Sana'a) now rely on solar power.

Aspects of policy and innovation in this area include:

  • Energy Resilience: Solar power serves as a lifeline, ensuring that healthcare and other basic functions can continue even if the national electricity infrastructure collapses.
  • International Organization Support: Organizations such as WHO, with support from the World Bank, have shifted from providing expensive and unsustainable emergency fuel to providing solar power systems for health facilities.
  • Private Sector and Community Adoption: The private sector has filled the gap in providing energy, clean water, and other services to those who can afford it, while the general public has widely adopted small-scale solar solutions on their own.
  • Economic Potential: There is recognition of the potential of renewable energy for long-term economic recovery and diversification, supported by a wide range of stakeholders.

  • Heritage Resilience & Community Strengthening: UNESCO has supported a project in Sana’a to build climate-resilient communities in the Old City. The project involves early warning systems for floods, community education, and disaster risk management to help protect both people and the historic fabric of Sana’a.
  • Cultural Heritage & Job Creation: Through UNESCO’s heritage conservation efforts (with EU and other partners), the Old City of Sana’a is not only being preserved but also used to generate livelihood opportunities for local youth. For example, thousands of young Yemenis have been trained and employed in restoring and maintaining historic buildings.
  • Sustainable Traditional Architecture: The old city of Sana’a is noted for its eco-friendly, climate-adapted architecture: traditional buildings are designed to reuse water (e.g., drainage from ablution water and rain) for neighborhood green spaces, demonstrating an early form of “green architecture.”

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Sana'a and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

UNESCO and World Bank documents show Sana’a’s Old City has UNESCO-level heritage value but faces serious conservation and capacity gaps. Bandung’s active heritage and creative-city networks (and its recent UNESCO-related initiatives) create a platform for knowledge exchange in restoration techniques, community-based heritage tourism, and training programs for conservation trades.

Sana’a’s CDS and UNESCO resilience projects identify flood, water stress, and infrastructure fragility as priorities; Bandung and other Asia–Africa cities participating in the Bandung City Network symposium are already exchanging best practices on urban resilience and climate adaptation — these topics are natural areas for technical cooperation, joint proposals to donors, and peer learning.

Bandung’s convening role in Asia–Africa city networks (and its push to nominate Asia–Africa-related sites to UNESCO programs) means it can host or co-design thematic exchanges (culture, creative economy, Memory of the World nominations) that include Sana’a and other KAA cities. This strengthens people-to-people links and opens channels for multilateral cultural projects.

The World Bank/Cities Alliance CDS for Sana’a documents institutional gaps (water, waste, informal settlements). Bandung’s urban planning units and universities (and its smart city/digital governance programs) could partner on pilot projects, staff exchanges, or joint capacity-building to improve service delivery models adapted to Sana’a’s context.
