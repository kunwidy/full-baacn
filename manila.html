Manila

14.941.953 Resident

Philippines

14,5995°N, 120,9842°E

Population Characteristics:

Social characteristics of the population of Manila City reflects a unique blend of indigenous (Malay), Spanish, American and Chinese cultural influences, which is seen in various aspects of daily life.

Manila is known as one of the most densely populated megacities in the world, with a very high population density in the downtown area. This density creates a vibrant and bustling city life, but it also presents challenges such as traffic congestion, housing shortages, and the emergence of slums in some areas. The predominant religion in Manila and the Philippines as a whole is Roman Catholicism, practiced by over 80% of the population. Religion plays a significant role in social and cultural life, with numerous historic churches and vibrant religious celebrations. Filipinos are known for their warmth, friendliness, and optimism. Despite facing various social and economic challenges, the people of Manila demonstrate great resilience in the face of life's hardships.

City's Unique Identity:

Cultural icons in Manila are symbols, objects, or practices that represent the identity, history, and values ​​of the Filipino people within the city's context.

The Jeepney is a unique and ubiquitous mode of public transportation in Manila and across the Philippines, creatively adapted from surplus US military jeeps after WorldWar II. It has evolved into an enduring cultural icon, known for its vibrant, flamboyant decoration and crowded seating.More than just transport, it symbolizes Filipino ingenuity,resilience, and artistic expression, earning its title as the "king of the road."Although modern transport is changing the landscape, the jeepney remains a powerful, recognizable emblem of daily life and popular culture in the city.

Dr. José Rizal is universally recognized as the national hero of the Philippines and a central cultural icon in Manila. His execution by Spanish authorities in the area now known as Rizal Park galvanized the Philippine Revolution. The Black Nazarene , a dark wooden statue of Jesus Christhoused at the Quiapo Church in Manila, isa powerful religious and cultural icon for millionsof Filipino Catholics.The annualtranslationprocession, where the statue is carried through thestreets, is one of the largest and mostintense religious events in the country, drawing millionsof barefoot devotees. This icon represents deep faith,devotion, and the resilience of the common people,showcasing a unique and fervent aspect of Filipino/popular religiosity.

Landmarks in Manila are specific physical sites or structures that serve as prominent points of orientation and hold significant historical, architectural, or geographical importance.

  • Intramuros is a major historical landmark that defines the old center of Manila. Enclosed by massive stone walls and moats, this district was the seat of Spanish colonial power for over 300 years. It is a tangible link to the city's past, featuring cobblestone streets, preserved Spanish-era architecture, and significant sites like Fort Santiago and the Manila Cathedral. As a landmark, Intramuros provides a physical map of Manila's colonial history and is an essential site for understanding the nation's heritage.
  • Rizal Park (Luneta) is not only a large urban green space but also a defining geographical and historical landmark of Manila along the bay. Its primary feature is the Rizal Monument, a towering structure marking the national hero's burial site. The park serves as a physical point of orientation in the city and is the location where Philippine independence was declared, making it a pivotal historical landmark that attracts both tourists and locals for recreation and national events.
  • Located within Intramuros, San Agustin Church is recognized as the oldest stone church in the Philippines and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Its unique Baroque architecture is a physical testament to Spanish craftsmanship and local resilience, having survived numerous disasters. The building itself is a landmark of architectural significance, representing the deep establishment of Roman Catholicism and colonial-era construction in the region.

  • Metro Manila Film Festival (MMFF) : An annual film festival that focuses on Filipino-produced films and runs from Christmas Day to early January.
  • Aliwan Fiesta : One of the biggest summer events in the capital region, co-organized by the Manila Broadcasting Company and the Cultural Center of the Philippines.
  • Caracol Festival : A street festival in Makati held every February to raise awareness about the environment and cultural heritage through unique costumes and dances.
  • Taste of Manila : A Filipino culinary and cultural festival held in Toronto, Canada instead of Manila, which aims to unite the Filipino-Canadian community.
  • Moriones Festival : A very dramatic and lively festival held in the province of Marinduque, which focuses on the story of Saint Longinus and uses Roman soldier costumes.
  • Pineapple Festival : An annual festival held in Daet district, which celebrates the region's unique pineapples through a colorful parade.

Culinary in Manila is very diverse, including authentic Filipino dishes like adobo, lechon, and kare-kare, as well as street food like kwek-kwek and unique drinks like halo-halo and magic water. In addition, Manila also boasts Spanish and Chinese culinary influences, which can be enjoyed in dishes like paella and dim sum.

The main local languages ​​in Manila are Tagalog, which is the basis for the national language of the Philippines, Filipino. Therefore, Manila's population largely speaks Tagalog and Filipino, both of which are very similar.

The Role of Cities in a National or Regional Context:

  • Center of Government and Politics: As the capital, Manila is the administrative center where the main government institutions and political headquarters are located. All major political decisions and national administration originate from this city.
  • Economic and Financial Center: Metro Manila (the National Capital Region) contributes a significant percentage of the nation's GDP (approximately 52.5% of GDP comes from the Manila Bay watershed). The city is a major business center, with a significant financial district in Makati, as well as a center for commerce, industry (manufacturing, food processing), and services. The Philippine Stock Exchange is also located here.
  • Social, Cultural, and Educational Center: Manila is the center of the Philippines' social and cultural life, home to renowned educational institutions, museums, historical sites (such as Intramuros and the Manila Cathedral), and a diverse cultural heritage. The city is a melting pot of ethnic groups and cultures from across the country.
  • Transportation and Logistics Hub: The Port of Manila is one of the busiest ports in the country and a major gateway for international trade, while its international airport connects the Philippines to the rest of the world.

City Development Focus:

The development of tourism in Manila City shows strong and positive recovery trends, with national tourism revenues surpassing pre-pandemic levels by early 2025 and promising growth projectionsAlthough the Philippines overall records lower levels of tourist arrivals than its Southeast Asian neighbors, the industry remains a major contributor to GDP and a vital source of employment.

The Philippine tourism industry, including Manila, has shown promising growth since 2020, with tourist arrivals and revenues rising rapidly. The tourism sector is projected to contribute PHP 2.7 trillion to the Philippine economy by 2025. In 2024, revenue from foreign tourists will reach approximately USD 13.1 billion. The development and modernization of facilities, such as tourist information centers and well-maintained pedestrian paths in historical areas such as Intramuros, improve visitor comfort.

  • Air Quality Commitment: The City of Manila has committed to aligning climate change and air pollution policies by 2030 to maximize the number of people breathing clean air. This is part of the global BreatheLife 2030 initiative, demonstrating a strong focus on public health and the urban environment.
  • New Manila Development: To alleviate the extreme overcrowding in the current capital, the Philippine government (not just the city government) is in the process of building a new city approximately 120 km from Manila. This ambitious project is designed to accommodate up to 2 million people and is expected to divert population pressure from congested Manila, currently the world's most densely populated city in terms of density per square kilometer.
  • Transportation Modernization: Efforts and discussions are underway to modernize the transportation system, including the elimination of outdated jeepneys and the introduction of more modern and inclusive vehicles. This aims to address Metro Manila's severe congestion and air pollution problems.
  • Smart City Initiatives: The Philippines, including Metro Manila, is exploring various smart city initiatives to improve the efficiency of public services and infrastructure, such as those supported by related US government programs.

Manila’s environmental and social initiatives have generated measurable public-good benefits in recent years. The national and local effort to rehabilitate Manila Bay, led by the Department of Environment and Natural Resources, has restored shorelines, reduced pollution in key esteros, and mobilized community and private-sector participation in coastal cleanups, improving coastal water quality and local livelihoods tied to fisheries and tourism.

At the metropolitan level, the MMDA Master Plan and related river-rehabilitation programs have prioritized cleaning waterways, flood mitigation, and urban greening, producing safer public spaces and reducing health risks from contaminated water and unmanaged waste in vulnerable communities.

National climate planning and resilience programs, coordinated by the Climate Change Commission and development partners, support community resilience, technical capacity building, and finance mechanisms (e.g., People’s Survival Fund and CORE program) that help cities like Manila adapt to sea-level rise, storms, and other climate hazards while creating jobs in green sectors.

Opportunities for Collaboration:

Cooperation between Manila and Bandung holds significant promise to deepen social, economic, and cultural ties.

Bandung can learn from Manila’s coastal rehabilitation experiences (and vice versa for tropical “sponge” flood-management adaptations). Joint pilots could adapt river clean-up, estero management, and nature-based stormwater measures to local conditions and share monitoring methodologies.

Manila’s large-scale cleanup campaigns and MMDA policies offer lessons on public–private partnerships for waste collection and recycling. Bandung (with its creative economy and MSME base) and Manila could co-develop community recycling enterprises and technical exchanges on informal-sector inclusion.

Through national programs (CCC’s CORE and People’s Survival Fund) and donor projects, Manila can partner with Bandung and KAA cities on resilience training, early-warning systems, and community adaptation projects—pooling resources for multilateral proposals to GEF, ADB, or Cities Alliance.

The Philippines, officially known as the Republic of the Philippines, is an archipelagic country in Southeast Asia. Located in the western Pacific Ocean, it consists of 7,641 islands, with a total area of roughly 300,000 square kilometers, which are broadly categorized in three main geographical divisions from north to south: Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao.
